Recommendation

Not at this time, and probably not at all during the ketamine restart period. A 7.5 mg mirtazapine retrial is pharmacologically defensible — at this dose the drug is mostly H1 antihistamine (the part that helped Dad's sleep last fall), with the noradrenergic activation that produced the agitation at 30–45 mg emerging much less. But three Dad-specific factors make this the wrong moment to test the retrial: (1) timing — adding mirtazapine simultaneously with the IV ketamine restart on 5/18 and the doxepin uptitration in progress creates an unacceptable confound for attributing what's working; (2) REM-sleep concerns — mirtazapine has been documented to induce or exacerbate dream-enactment behavior in patients with parkinsonian features, and Dad's formal RBD diagnosis + pending neurology workup make this a real flag the family's prior literature review #11 also weighted; (3) better alternatives are available — the doxepin uptitration to 10–12 mg HS already underway is the right bridge, and a dual orexin receptor antagonist (DORA) would be the more evidence-supported addition if doxepin alone proves insufficient. If mirtazapine is eventually pursued (after the ketamine response is stabilized and other options have been tried), the conditions should include neurology clearance given the RBD, a baseline ECG given LAFB + bradycardia, starting at 3.75 mg (half a 7.5 mg tablet) for 5–7 nights before considering 7.5 mg, and a strict monitoring protocol with clear stopping rules. Mom's recall of sleep benefit last fall is real and consistent with mirtazapine's H1 effect — the question isn't whether mirtazapine can help sleep, it's whether the timing and risk profile are right now.

Why this matters for Dad specifically

Dr. R raised low-dose mirtazapine as a "consider" option in the 5/16 plan, alongside continuing the doxepin uptitration. Mom recalls that the prior trial (7/23/2025 – 10/1/2025) helped with sleep before the agitation emerged — and that recall is accurate. The chart confirms a real 2+ month trial: 30 mg starting 7/23, refill at 30 mg on 9/4, dose increased to 45 mg on 9/9, last fill 10/1 at 30 mg, discontinued ~October 2025. The 30 → 45 → 30 → stop pattern suggests the agitation emerged with the dose bump to 45 mg and persisted on the return to 30 mg until discontinuation — pharmacologically consistent with the noradrenergic-activation mechanism that becomes clinically meaningful in the 15–30 mg range. The proposed retrial at 7.5 mg would, in theory, capture the H1-mediated sleep benefit without recruiting the noradrenergic activation. That theory is reasonable but not directly tested in clinical trials of agitation-prone patients. The family's prior literature review #11 (4/20/2026) weighed this exact question and concluded "off the table for now... Only consider if all other options fail." Three factors make that conclusion still right today: the timing relative to the IV ketamine restart, the specific RBD-related concerns, and the availability of better-evidenced alternatives (doxepin uptitration already underway; DORA class available as the next step if needed).

The evidence
What might support a 7.5 mg retrial What argues against — in Dad's specific profile
Mom's recall of sleep benefit is accurate and grounded in real pharmacology. Mirtazapine has very high affinity for H1 histamine receptors (Ki ~0.14 nM) — at low doses (≤7.5 mg), H1 occupancy approaches saturation while the noradrenergic alpha-2 effects remain minimal. The Karsten 2017 RCT in healthy volunteers showed 7.5 mg significantly increased total sleep time by ~30 minutes, reduced awakenings by 35–40%, and specifically enhanced deep (slow-wave) sleep. Sleep benefit appears on the first night. Timing is the dominant concern. The IV ketamine restart on 5/18 is the highest-priority intervention and the main expected driver of sleep, anxiety, and mood improvement over the next 1–2 weeks. The doxepin uptitration to 10–12 mg HS is already in progress as the bridge strategy. Adding mirtazapine simultaneously means three pharmacological changes in the same window — which destroys the family's ability to attribute any change (positive or negative) to a specific agent. The handout #08 framing applies here: don't run multiple pharmacological transitions concurrently in a 76-year-old.
The 7.5 mg vs. 30–45 mg pharmacological distinction is real. The dose-response is continuous, not biphasic, but the clinical inflection point where noradrenergic activation becomes meaningful is in the 15–30 mg range. At 7.5 mg, the noradrenergic contribution is expected to be minimal. The family's framing — "7.5 mg captures only H1/5-HT2A effects without the noradrenergic rebound" — is a reasonable simplification. The RBD/REM concern is meaningful and underappreciated. Mirtazapine has been directly implicated in inducing or exacerbating RBD, particularly in patients with parkinsonian features (Onofrj 2003 case series — four patients with parkinsonism developed RBD shortly after mirtazapine initiation at 15 mg, with resolution on discontinuation). Antidepressants as a class increase REM sleep without atonia even in patients without clinical RBD (McCarter 2015). Dad has formal RBD (G47.52) since 2017, prior drug-induced parkinsonism from aripiprazole, and a neurology referral pending June. Postuma 2013 found that patients with antidepressant-associated RBD still showed prodromal-neurodegeneration markers — suggesting antidepressants may unmask underlying pathology rather than just cause de novo REM-sleep changes. Whether 7.5 mg carries the same REM risk as 15 mg is not directly studied.
Weight/appetite effect could be beneficial. Mirtazapine reliably increases appetite and weight gain — potentially helpful given Dad's documented unintentional weight loss (R63.4, 183 → 169 lbs over 2.5 years). At 30 mg in a fibromyalgia trial, weight gain was 4.5 kg over 13 weeks; at 15 mg, 2 kg; at placebo, 1 kg. At 7.5 mg the effect is attenuated but present. The activation-intolerance pattern is broader than dose. Dad has shown paradoxical or activation responses to multiple agents across multiple mechanisms: aripiprazole akathisia at even 1 mg, mirtazapine agitation at 30–45 mg, olanzapine "agitation before benefit," brexpiprazole self-discontinued for balance. This breadth suggests an intrinsic vulnerability — possibly related to the underlying neurology picture under evaluation — that isn't cleanly dose-dependent. The pharmacological argument for 7.5 mg avoiding the noradrenergic mechanism is reasonable but unproven in the specific scenario of an activation-prone patient retrying after a higher-dose failure.
Sleep architecture profile is favorable. Unlike SSRIs and SNRIs, mirtazapine does not significantly suppress REM sleep at either low or standard doses. The slow-wave sleep enhancement is the specific benefit that the Karsten 2017 trial captured. Better-evidenced alternatives are available. The AGS Beers Criteria companion document explicitly states there is "insufficient evidence to recommend mirtazapine" for insomnia in older adults, while endorsing low-dose doxepin (up to 6 mg) and dual orexin receptor antagonists (DORAs — lemborexant, suvorexant, daridorexant) as safer alternatives with evidence of effectiveness. Doxepin uptitration to 10–12 mg HS is already in motion. If that proves insufficient after the ketamine course stabilizes, a DORA is the more evidence-supported next step than mirtazapine retrial.
— The standing serotonergic stack would become three agents (duloxetine 60 mg + doxepin 6–12 mg HS + mirtazapine 7.5 mg HS), with trazodone 50 mg as a PRN backup (prescription active; one dose taken 5/15; the current plan is doxepin uptitration instead of regular trazodone use). At low doses, individual serotonergic contributions are modest, but cumulative burden on a daily basis is non-trivial and serotonin syndrome (though rare) is a real consideration. The FDA mirtazapine label explicitly lists SNRIs (duloxetine) as drugs that increase serotonin syndrome risk when combined.
— Cumulative H1 antagonism and sedation with doxepin 10–12 mg + mirtazapine 7.5 mg + melatonin 3–5 mg is additive. In a 76-year-old with brain fog (R41.89), chronic fatigue (R53.82), and who drives independently, cumulative sedative load matters — particularly for the morning after dosing. Both doxepin and mirtazapine are H1 antagonists; both have low but non-zero anticholinergic activity; both have meaningful next-day half-life.
— No published RCT for low-dose retrial after prior high-dose agitation. The pharmacological reasoning that 7.5 mg avoids the noradrenergic mechanism is plausible but not directly tested in patients with Dad's specific history. The Karsten 2017 trial used healthy volunteers without prior mirtazapine intolerance. The published success rate of 7.5 mg retrials in agitation-prone patients is unknown.
— Cardiac caution warranted. Mirtazapine doesn't prolong QTc meaningfully at standard doses, and risk at 7.5 mg is negligible — but the Dutch consensus on antidepressant ECG monitoring recommends baseline ECG when ≥2 risk factors are present (age >65, cardiac disease, concomitant QTc-prolonging drugs). Dad has age >65 + LAFB + bradycardia HR ~57. A baseline ECG before initiation is reasonable, even though the absolute risk is low.
Strength of evidence against retrial at this time: ★★★★
Convergent reasoning rather than a single decisive trial: timing relative to ketamine restart (handout #08 framing); RBD risk specifically documented for mirtazapine in parkinsonism (Onofrj 2003; McCarter 2015; Postuma 2013); activation-intolerance pattern that spans multiple agents; availability of better-evidenced alternatives (low-dose doxepin already uptitrating; DORAs as next step per AGS Beers alternatives). The pharmacological case for 7.5 mg being qualitatively different from 30 mg is real, but the family's prior literature review #11 weighed this exact question and concluded "off the table for now." Nothing in the new evidence changes that conclusion for the current moment.
