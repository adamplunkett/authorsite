Why Cymbalta + Wellbutrin + Lemborexant is the strongest evidence-based configuration

Four OpenEvidence consultations completed over the past three days (#15b on lower-dose Lexapro re-trial; #16 on Lexapro + Wellbutrin combination; #17 head-to-head bupropion vs divalproex; #18 on concurrent medication changes in the ketamine window) converge on the same conclusion: preserving the duloxetine background and adding bupropion as augmentation is the highest-evidence configuration across every axis evaluated. Each of these consultations is summarized in the daily update handout (#20); their key findings are integrated below.

The evidence picture, condensed
  • Documented February 2026 ketamine response was on Cymbalta + brexpiprazole. Dad's own N=1 prior responder data is the single strongest predictor of ketamine re-response. Removing Cymbalta removes the antidepressant platform that backed the documented response. This is the most important single fact for the operative decision.
  • Del Casale 2025 (JAMA Psychiatry, n=55,480, propensity-matched): SNRI + esketamine outperformed SSRI + esketamine on mortality (5.3% vs 9.1%), hospitalization, and depression relapse (14.8% vs 21.2%). Mechanism favors SNRI synergy with ketamine (norepinephrine neuron firing is part of ketamine's mechanism). The single largest dataset on this specific question, and it points against the switch direction.
  • OPTIMUM 2023 (NEJM, n=619 adults ≥60): Bupropion augmentation produced 28.2% remission — comparable to aripiprazole augmentation (28.9%), superior to switching antidepressants (19.3%). This is the direct evidence for the augmentation strategy in geriatric TRD. Divalproex has no comparable evidence and is not in any major guideline for unipolar TRD augmentation.
  • Bupropion vs divalproex on every axis for Dad's specific profile (per OE #17 head-to-head): bupropion 7.5/10, divalproex 2.5/10. Bupropion wins on TRD efficacy evidence, drug-induced parkinsonism risk, neurology-eval confounding, ketamine compatibility, cognitive effects, and apathy phenotype fit. Bupropion's only weaker axis (fall risk) is dose-dependent and mitigated at 150 mg in patients without significant prior fall history.
  • ASCP 2026 Deprescribing Consensus: 100% expert agreement on "only one psychiatric medication change at a time, whenever feasible." AGS Beers Criteria: flags ≥3 concurrent CNS-active agents as potentially inappropriate. The proposed Lexapro + Depakote + Lemborexant package would reach or exceed that threshold; Cymbalta + Wellbutrin + Lemborexant stays within it.
  • AASM RBD management guidance (Howell 2023): SSRIs can induce or exacerbate RBD; bupropion is among the antidepressants with lower serotonergic profile and is the recommended consideration in RBD patients who need an antidepressant. Dad has formal RBD since 10/2017 — 8.5 years. The Lexapro switch is the wrong direction relative to AASM-aligned management; bupropion augmentation is the right direction.
  • Holper 2020 reanalysis with age as a covariate: No antidepressant provides balanced benefit (efficacy exceeding adverse events) in patients aged 70+. Population-level finding that argues against investing energy in switching antidepressants in this age group when no AD switch is likely to be the load-bearing intervention. Ketamine is the load-bearing intervention; bupropion augmentation is the supportive add.
  • Lemborexant for sleep (per OE #13): Night-1 effective in adults ≥55 (WASO reduction of 33 min at 5 mg, 42 min at 10 mg vs placebo on nights 1–2); seamless single-night substitution from doxepin; no rebound insomnia or withdrawal on discontinuation. Julane has endorsed lemborexant specifically as the sleep-maintenance addition. This component of the proposed package is well-supported in both family-preferred and currently-proposed configurations.
Addressing Dr. Chilton's stated concern directly

Dr. Chilton's reasoning for the Lexapro switch was that ketamine paired with an SSRI is safer than ketamine paired with an SNRI, and that Dad needs to be less acutely anxious before the next session. Both pieces of that reasoning are addressable without switching antidepressants:

  • On SSRI-safer-than-SNRI for ketamine background: Del Casale 2025's 55,480-patient propensity-matched dataset points the opposite direction. SNRI + ketamine outperformed SSRI + ketamine on every major outcome. The biology supports SNRI synergy with ketamine (NE neuron firing is integral to ketamine's mechanism). If the operative concern is ketamine compatibility, Cymbalta is the better-evidenced background, not the agent to switch away from.
  • On reducing acute anxiety before the next session: SSRI anxiolytic effect takes 4–8 weeks to manifest. The Lexapro switch in the next 3 weeks cannot achieve the acute-anxiety reduction Dr. Chilton wants — even with an ideal cross-titration. By contrast, bupropion's effect on apathy and energy can be detectable within 1–2 weeks; and integration work with Julane in the 3-week window is the most evidence-supported acute-anxiety lever. The right answer to "reduce acute anxiety before next session" is set/setting work + behavioral preparation + (potentially) single-dose pre-session lorazepam, not an SSRI switch.

If Tier 1 isn't agreed: the fallback positions

The family-preferred configuration (Tier 1) is the strongest evidence-based ask, but if Dr. Rubin is committed to switching antidepressants, the fallback positions below preserve as much of the evidence-supported structure as possible while accommodating the constraint.

Tier 2 — Lexapro + Wellbutrin + Lemborexant

If the Cymbalta → Lexapro switch is non-negotiable, the single most important substitution to fight for is bupropion in the augmentation slot in place of divalproex. OPTIMUM data, drug-induced-parkinsonism considerations, neurology-eval confounding, ketamine compatibility, and apathy-phenotype fit all favor bupropion overwhelmingly (per OE #17, 7.5/10 vs 2.5/10).

  • Cross-titration over 6–8 weeks, not 3. The 3-week timeline overlapping the ketamine restart is the operative concern. Published cross-titration principles in elderly anxiety-predominant TRD support 6–8 weeks minimum.
  • CYP2C19 genotyping ordered before any Lexapro dose decision. Dad's historical effective dose was 30 mg (per 4/21 audit) — well above the FDA geriatric cap of 10 mg. If Dad is a CYP2C19 ultrarapid metabolizer, that explains the historical need for a supratherapeutic dose and would inform whether to consider a higher restart dose with cardiac monitoring (per Jang 2025).
  • Single-night doxepin → lemborexant substitution. Per OE #13, both produce first-night WASO effects; seamless transition expected.
  • Bupropion XL 150 mg AM as augmentation, started 2–3 weeks before the June 8 ketamine restart or after the first 2–3 ketamine sessions per Tier 3 sequencing.
Tier 3 — Lexapro + bupropion + lemborexant with deferred bupropion start

If Dr. Rubin insists on the full Lexapro + augmentation + sleep restructuring all happening at once, the most defensible damage-control configuration is:

  • Lexapro switch and lemborexant substitution start now (cross-titrated over 6–8 weeks).
  • Bupropion XL 150 mg added after the first 2–3 ketamine sessions — preserves clean attribution for the ketamine response read; lets the SSRI switch settle before another new agent is layered on.
  • Bupropion replaces divalproex in the augmentation slot regardless of timing. This substitution is the single most evidence-supported piece of the family's preferences and should be the non-negotiable on the augmentation question.
  • CYP2C19 genotyping before Lexapro dose is set.
  • Trazodone PRN maintained; melatonin 3 mg HS continues.
What should never be on the table
  • Divalproex without a clearly stated indication. No RCT evidence for unipolar TRD augmentation at any age; not in any major guideline for this indication; documented drug-induced parkinsonism risk that is particularly hazardous in Dad's highest-risk Arnaldi profile; raises doxepin levels 2.2× via UGT inhibition; possible ketamine response attenuation per Xu 2015. See handout #01.
  • Lexapro at >10 mg without CYP2C19 genotyping first. Geriatric cap is 10 mg. If exceeding the cap is contemplated based on Dad's historical 30 mg need, pharmacogenomic data should inform the decision; QT-prolongation is dose-dependent and Dad's LAFB + bradycardia warrants caution.
  • Removing trazodone PRN. Used infrequently; Chilton-cleared; serves no purpose to remove.
  • A 3-week cross-titration in the pre-ketamine window. Published principles in elderly anxiety-predominant TRD support 6–8 weeks. The current proposed timeline lands the duloxetine-withdrawal-anxiety window and the Lexapro-start-activation window directly on the ketamine restart.

Operational details — dosing, monitoring, reversal triggers

If Tier 1 (Cymbalta + Wellbutrin + Lemborexant) is agreed
  1. Cymbalta 60 mg daily — continue unchanged.
  2. Bupropion XL 150 mg AM, starting now (or up to 2–3 weeks before the June 8 ketamine restart). Hold at 150 mg for at least 4 weeks before any consideration of uptitration, given the CYP2D6 interaction with duloxetine (mutual modest elevation of each other's levels). Do not exceed 300 mg/day given the interaction.
  3. Doxepin 6 mg HS off → lemborexant 5 mg HS, single-night substitution. Per OE #13, no expected gap in sleep coverage. If 5 mg is tolerated and benefit incomplete, escalate to 10 mg at the 7–10 day reassessment.
  4. Trazodone 50 mg HS PRN — continue, used as needed.
  5. Melatonin 3 mg HS — continue (prescribed for RBD since 2017).
  6. Baseline ECG before bupropion start. Repeat ECG at 1–2 weeks if any cardiac symptoms emerge.
  7. Weekly check-ins for first 4 weeks: phone or in-person with Mom or Dad, focused on gait/balance, orthostatic vitals (lying-to-standing BP), agitation/insomnia, any fall or near-fall, mood ratings (PHQ-9 baseline + at week 4).
Reversal triggers (pre-committed, agreed on the call)

These should be stated explicitly to Dr. Rubin on the call so they're on the record and the family doesn't have to argue them later. Any of these in the first 4 weeks of bupropion start prompts stopping bupropion and reassessing:

  • Any new tremor, rigidity, or gait deterioration (treat as possible drug-induced movement effect)
  • Akathisia, persistent restlessness, or agitation that doesn't resolve in 48 hours of dose-holding
  • Any fall, including without injury
  • Persistent insomnia not responsive to taking the bupropion earlier in the day
  • Hypertensive surge or BP elevation above 140/90
  • Seizure or seizure-like activity
  • PHQ-9 increase ≥5 points from pre-bupropion baseline
  • Any psychotic symptom, severe anxiety attack, or cognitive deterioration beyond baseline
Sleep-substitution-specific monitoring
  • Night 1 self-report: sleep onset latency, awakenings, morning state, any dream content
  • Week 1 check: morning grogginess (relevant for driving), any new dream-enactment episodes, any sleep paralysis or hypnagogic phenomena, balance/falls. The DORA-class FDA-labeled signals (sleep paralysis 1.3–1.6%, hypnagogic hallucinations 0.1–0.7%, nightmares 0.9–2.2%) are uncommon but real
  • Discontinuation trigger for lemborexant: any new RBD episode (or increase in frequency), any sleep paralysis or hypnagogic hallucination, any fall, persistent morning grogginess affecting daytime function

Key studies supporting the recommendation

The references below anchor the specific evidence claims in the comparison table and the synthesis. Each citation summarized for how it applies to Dad's case.

Augmentation evidence in geriatric TRD
Lenze EJ, Mulsant BH, Roose SP et al. Antidepressant Augmentation versus Switch in Treatment-Resistant Geriatric Depression (OPTIMUM).
N Engl J Med 2023;388:1067–1079.
Key findingRCT of 619 adults ≥60 with TRD. Bupropion augmentation: 28.2% remission. Aripiprazole augmentation: 28.9% (comparable). Switching to bupropion: 19.3% (worst arm). Falls higher in bupropion augmentation (0.55/patient/10 weeks) vs aripiprazole (0.33).
Kim HK et al. Moderators of Antidepressant Augmentation Versus Switch in the OPTIMUM RCT.
Br J Psychiatry 2025.
Key findingAugmentation was superior to switching specifically in patients with fewer than three prior adequate antidepressant trials (moderation coefficient b = −1.6, p = 0.033). In patients with many prior trials, the augmentation advantage attenuates. Relevant for Dad's history of many prior agents.
Ketamine background and SNRI vs SSRI pairing
Del Casale A et al. Esketamine Combined With SSRI or SNRI for Treatment-Resistant Depression.
JAMA Psychiatry 2025;82:810–817.
Key findingPropensity-matched cohort, n=55,480. SNRI + esketamine outperformed SSRI + esketamine on 5-year mortality (5.3% vs 9.1%), hospitalization, and relapse (14.8% vs 21.2%). The single largest dataset on this specific question; argues for preserving the SNRI background through ketamine.
Phillips JL et al. Single, Repeated, and Maintenance Ketamine Infusions for TRD.
Am J Psychiatry 2019;176:401–409.
Key finding~46% relapse without maintenance vs ~27% with scheduled maintenance. Documents importance of stable background medication for assessing ketamine response and planning maintenance.
Drug-induced parkinsonism risk with divalproex
Brugger F, Bhatia KP, Besag FM. Valproate-Associated Parkinsonism: A Critical Review.
CNS Drugs 2016;30:527–540.
Key findingPrevalence 1.4–75% depending on assessment; dose-independent (occurs at therapeutic and subtherapeutic levels); insidious onset; recovery often prolonged and sometimes incomplete. 116 published cases reviewed.
Belete D et al. Association Between Antiepileptic Drugs and Incident Parkinson Disease.
JAMA Neurology 2023;80:183–187.
Key findingUK Biobank nested case-control (1,433 PD cases, 8,598 controls). Sodium valproate robustly associated with incident PD; persisted after adjusting for epilepsy and after excluding prescriptions within 5 years of PD diagnosis. Supports unmasking-of-subclinical-disease mechanism.
REM Sleep Behavior Disorder and antidepressant choice
Howell M et al. Management of REM Sleep Behavior Disorder: AASM Clinical Practice Guideline.
J Clin Sleep Med 2023;19:759–768.
Key findingSSRIs can induce or exacerbate RBD. Bupropion is among the antidepressants with lower serotonergic profile and is the AASM consideration when an antidepressant is needed in RBD patients. Directly relevant to Dad's 8.5-year formal RBD.
Polypharmacy / concurrent medication changes
Goldberg JF et al. Recommendations for the Deprescribing of Psychotropic Medications (ASCP Consensus).
JAMA Network Open 2026;9:e260043.
Key finding100% expert agreement (95% CI 93–100%): "Only one deprescribing change at a time, whenever feasible." Directly speaks to the concurrent-change concern in the currently proposed package.
AGS Beers Criteria 2023 (companion update 2024).
AGS / J Am Geriatr Soc.
Key findingFlags ≥3 concurrent CNS-active agents as potentially inappropriate in older adults. The proposed Lexapro + Depakote + Lemborexant + ketamine package reaches or exceeds that threshold.
Lemborexant and sleep architecture
Rosenberg R et al. Comparison of Lemborexant With Placebo and Zolpidem (SUNRISE-1).
JAMA Network Open 2019;2:e1918254.
Key findingRCT, n=1,006 adults ≥55 with insomnia. Lemborexant 5 mg reduced WASO by 33 min vs placebo on nights 1–2; 10 mg by 42 min. Second-half-of-night WASO advantage over zolpidem ER present from night 1. No tolerance over 12 months in SUNRISE-2.
Late-life antidepressant net benefit
Holper L. Optimal Doses of Antidepressants in Dependence on Age (Bayesian network meta-analysis).
EClinicalMedicine 2020;18:100219.
Key findingNo antidepressant provided balanced benefit (efficacy exceeding adverse events) in patients aged 70 and over, because adverse events exceed efficacy at all doses in this age group. Argues against investing energy in switching antidepressants in this population.
