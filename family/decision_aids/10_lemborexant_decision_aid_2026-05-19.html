Recommendation (revised 5/19)

Lemborexant 5 mg HS is defensible — either as a seamless substitute for doxepin if doxepin is coming off, or as an addition for sleep-maintenance support if it stays. The 5/19 OpenEvidence consultation (#13) substantially flipped the original 5/15 against-position. The two strongest new findings: (1) lemborexant is night-1 effective in adults ≥55 — WASO reduction of 33 min (5 mg) and 42 min (10 mg) vs placebo on nights 1–2 in SUNRISE-1; second-half-of-night WASO benefit present from night 1, no accumulation period required; (2) the doxepin → lemborexant transition can be done seamlessly on a single night — both produce first-night WASO effects, no expected gap. No rebound insomnia, no withdrawal — can be stopped abruptly if it doesn't work or produces adverse effects, which makes the trial genuinely low-risk and reversible. PK interactions with duloxetine and low-dose doxepin are not significant (CYP3A4-metabolized; no inhibition from Dad's stack). The original 5/15 cautions are softer than first stated: RBD-specific clinical data is absent, but the mouse-tauopathy DORA-12 data actually point toward reduced dream-enactment (opposite the original concern); the FDA "don't combine with other hypnotics" guidance is regulatory caution rather than a strong PK signal at these doses; the REM-augmentation effect could theoretically increase RBD-episode opportunity but has not been demonstrated clinically. Operative position: if Rubin's proposal involves removing doxepin, lemborexant 5 mg HS is a defensible substitute. If doxepin stays, lemborexant can be added if sleep maintenance is the limiting symptom. Either way, melatonin 3 mg HS and trazodone PRN continue. Start at 5 mg HS; reassess at 7–10 days; escalate to 10 mg only if 5 mg is tolerated and benefit incomplete. Julane endorses the addition.

The evidence (revised 2026-05-19 with OE #13 findings)
What supports lemborexant in this profile What still warrants caution
Night-1 effective in adults ≥55. SUNRISE-1 (Rosenberg 2019, n=1,006, median age 63, 45% ≥65 years): on nights 1–2, lemborexant 5 mg reduced LPS by ~17 min and WASO by ~33 min vs placebo; 10 mg reduced LPS by ~20 min and WASO by ~42 min. Sustained through 12 months in SUNRISE-2 with no tolerance. Steady-state efficacy is essentially night 1 — no accumulation needed. RBD-specific clinical data: absent. SUNRISE trials excluded patients with significant parasomnias. The mouse-tauopathy DORA-12 data (Kam 2023) showed reduced dream-enactment behaviors — opposite direction of the original concern, but not human RBD data. Theoretical concern: lemborexant modestly increases REM sleep duration (a class effect of DORAs); more REM time could theoretically produce more episode opportunity, though this hasn't been demonstrated.
Second-half-of-night WASO benefit present from night 1. Distinguishing feature vs zolpidem ER. This is exactly the pattern Dad reports (waking unrested, can't return to sleep, early-morning awakening). Lemborexant 5 mg superior to zolpidem ER on second-half WASO by ~6.5 min on nights 1–2 and 10 mg superior by ~13.1 min. FDA-labeled narcolepsy-spectrum signals. Sleep paralysis 1.3% (5 mg) and 1.6% (10 mg) vs 0% placebo; hypnagogic hallucinations 0.1% (5 mg) and 0.7% (10 mg) vs 0% placebo; abnormal dreams/nightmares 0.9% (5 mg) and 2.2% (10 mg) vs 0.9% placebo; cataplexy-like leg weakness reported. These are uncommon but real; mechanism is REM-related and worth flagging in a patient with formal RBD even if mechanistically distinct from dream enactment.
Doxepin → lemborexant substitution is seamless. Both produce first-night WASO effects; lemborexant's night-1 effect size at least matches doxepin 6 mg. Single-night transition expected to maintain sleep-maintenance benefit. If Rubin's proposal includes removing doxepin, this substitution is operationally defensible without an interim gap. Removing doxepin is itself a separate question. The OE response on doxepin substitution doesn't endorse removing doxepin — it only addresses what happens if doxepin is being removed. If doxepin is working and is well-tolerated, the conservative move is to keep it and add lemborexant on top (Julane's recommendation). The doxepin-removal piece of Rubin's proposal still requires its own justification.
No rebound insomnia, no withdrawal. Lemborexant can be stopped abruptly with no rebound insomnia or withdrawal symptoms in either the 1-month or 12-month trials (per FDA label and SUNRISE-2 extension). This is the load-bearing point for the "low-risk reversible trial" framing — if it doesn't work or produces adverse effects, the trial is over the next morning. FDA "don't combine with other hypnotics" label. Regulatory caution rather than a specific PK warning. At low doses with no significant PK interaction expected, the practical concern is additive sedation if combined with doxepin 6 mg HS. Morning grogginess should be tracked carefully; if Dad still drives independently, fall and driving-impairment monitoring matters.
No significant PK interaction with current stack. Lemborexant is CYP3A4-metabolized. Duloxetine is not a CYP3A4 inhibitor (it's a CYP1A2 + CYP2D6 substrate; moderate CYP2D6 inhibitor). Doxepin at 6 mg is unlikely to meaningfully inhibit CYP3A4. Combination is pharmacologically manageable. Orexin-system involvement in synucleinopathies is the deeper theoretical question. Raheel 2024 review documents adaptive changes in orexin levels in early prodromal synucleinopathy. Lemborexant pharmacologically blocks both OX1R and OX2R. Whether this disrupts a compensatory adaptation or is benign is genuinely unknown. The U-M Movement Disorders evaluation in June would benefit from a stable baseline; introducing orexin antagonism before that evaluation is a small but real consideration.
Cardiac profile favorable for Dad. Lemborexant does not prolong QTc at doses up to 5× max recommended. Safe for bradycardia + LAFB. No meaningful effect on heart rate or conduction. Dose escalation timing. If 5 mg HS is partially effective, the typical practice is to wait 7–10 days before considering 10 mg. The 10 mg dose has marginally larger effect on WASO (~8 min additional on nights 1–2) but carries higher rates of somnolence (9.6% vs 6.9%) and slightly higher sleep paralysis (1.6% vs 1.3%). For Dad with bradycardia, the 5 mg starting dose is prudent.
Compatible with ketamine. No published interaction either direction. Orexin antagonism operates through lateral hypothalamic pathways, mechanistically distinct from ketamine's glutamatergic mechanism. Improved sleep itself may support ketamine response (sleep disruption is associated with poorer antidepressant outcomes generally). —
Julane endorses lemborexant. The integration provider with continuous longitudinal observation of Dad has specifically endorsed lemborexant as the appropriate sleep-maintenance addition. Her endorsement is structurally informative — she has the cleanest perspective on what's working in the broader integration of Dad's care. —
