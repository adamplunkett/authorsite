Recommendation

For the next 2–3 nights before the May 18 IV ketamine restart, the strongest move is to optimize within Dad's existing stack rather than add a new agent. Specifically: take trazodone at 75 mg HS each of the three bridge nights (a temporary uptitration from the usual 50 mg PRN — return to PRN dosing after the bridge), titrate melatonin from 3 → 5 mg HS (USP-verified, extended-release if available), continue doxepin 6 mg HS unchanged, and tighten sleep hygiene. Don't add ZZZquil, Benadryl, or Unisom — these are explicitly contraindicated in Dad's profile. Don't add klonopin — given the recent Ativan taper, ongoing driving, and klonopin's long half-life, it's not the right fit. Don't add zolpidem (Ambien) or eszopiclone (Lunesta) — fall risk and parasomnia exacerbation are real in his profile. The ketamine restart on 5/18 is itself a sleep intervention — in Dad's February-responder profile, sleep typically improves within the first 1–2 IV sessions.

Why this matters for Dad specifically

Dad called 5/15 asking for "a day or two of relief" — a real and important request after weeks of severe early-morning waking. But the question turns on three clinical facts about the next 72 hours: (1) any new sleep agent introduced now will have its effects entangled with the IV ketamine restart on 5/18 — the family loses the ability to attribute what's working; (2) most candidate agents carry next-day cognitive or motor effects that are amplified by his age and ongoing driving; (3) the obvious "fast" options (klonopin or restarting Ativan) would undo the gains of the recently completed Ativan taper. The right strategy for these 3 nights is to push as much benefit as possible from agents already in his stack at known-safe doses, tighten the non-pharmacologic factors, and steer clear of the OTC and prescription options that aren't right for his profile. The broader 1–3 month sleep landscape question is addressed in companion handout #12.

The evidence
What to do for the next 2–3 nights What NOT to add
Take trazodone 75 mg HS each of the three bridge nights (5/15, 5/16, 5/17) — a temporary uptitration from the usual 50 mg PRN. A modest dose jump that's incremental, not a category change. It stays well below the antidepressant range (150–400 mg), keeps the serotonergic burden manageable on top of duloxetine, and stays at the lower end of the QT-relevant dose curve for his LAFB + bradycardia profile. Taking it consistently across the three bridge nights (rather than as-needed) creates more reliable sleep architecture for that short window. After the bridge, return to the 50 mg PRN pattern (see handout #12 for the ongoing landscape). ZZZquil (diphenhydramine), Benadryl, Unisom (doxylamine) — even a single dose. Both are on the AGS Beers Criteria list as "avoid" in older adults. They are strongly anticholinergic, would worsen brain fog acutely, work against the cholinergic system the pending neurology workup is helping evaluate, carry urinary retention risk on top of his alfuzosin for BPH, and elevate fall risk. Tolerance also develops fast, so even 2–3 nights of relief degrades quickly.
Titrate melatonin from 3 mg to 5 mg HS, USP-verified product. Negligible drug-interaction risk in his stack. Melatonin acts on MT1/MT2 receptors (circadian and sleep-promoting), a mechanism complementary to doxepin's H1 antagonism — so it's not pharmacologically redundant. AASM 2023 supports immediate-release melatonin for RBD (dosing 3 mg titrated up to 15 mg). Older adults have endogenous melatonin decline, so titrating upward has a clinical rationale beyond placebo effect. If a verified extended-release formulation is available, it's preferred for the sleep-maintenance picture. Klonopin (clonazepam) — even at 0.25 mg HS. Not the right fit. The Ativan taper was recently completed and that progress is worth protecting — reintroducing a benzodiazepine, including a longer-acting one like klonopin, would mean redoing that work later, and klonopin's long half-life would make a future taper trajectory harder than the recent one. The same 30–40 hour half-life also means measurable next-day cognitive and motor effects, which is a real safety consideration for an ongoing driver in his age group. Geriatric prescribing guidelines (AGS Beers) flag it as not appropriate at his age. The AASM RBD guideline itself prefers melatonin over clonazepam in older adults whose broader neurologic picture is being evaluated, which is the situation here.
Continue doxepin 6 mg HS unchanged. Don't titrate even briefly. The AGS Beers Criteria explicitly flag doxepin >6 mg/day as having strong anticholinergic properties — at 3–6 mg the drug is highly H1-selective and clean; at 10 mg it starts engaging muscarinic, alpha-1, and serotonin reuptake systems. The 6 mg dose already provides near-maximal H1 occupancy, so the uptitration buys minimal sleep benefit at meaningful side-effect cost. Zolpidem (Ambien), eszopiclone (Lunesta), zaleplon. The Z-drug class roughly triples hip-fracture risk in older adults. Parasomnia exacerbation — including sleepwalking and complex sleep behaviors — is well-documented and is a specific concern in a patient with formal RBD where the REM-atonia mechanism is already abnormal. Even 2–3 nights carry non-trivial risk of a fall, a complex sleep event, or a cognitive event. Eszopiclone has FDA approval for sleep maintenance, but the class-level safety concerns don't disappear at the lower dose.
Strict sleep hygiene for the three bridge nights. Consistent wake time (set an alarm even if he wakes earlier — getting up at the same hour anchors circadian rhythm). No screens after 8 PM. Cool, dark room. No caffeine after noon. No daytime napping (preserves sleep pressure for the night). Avoid catastrophizing about each night's sleep — the anxiety about not sleeping is a recognized driver of waking. Behavioral discipline is the single highest-yield intervention available in the next 72 hours. Hydroxyzine 25–50 mg PRN. Beers-listed first-generation antihistamine with substantial anticholinergic burden. Cumulative load adds to Dad's brain fog and may interact unfavorably with his possible Lewy prodrome. Sleep benefit is not superior to trazodone at the doses commonly used.
Morning bright light exposure. 15–20 minutes of outdoor light in the first hour after waking helps reset circadian phase. For someone whose specific complaint is 4 AM waking — a pattern often associated with circadian phase advance in older adults and depression — this is a directly targeted intervention with no side effects. Mirtazapine 7.5 mg trial. Dad's prior mirtazapine trial (12/2025) caused agitation — a recognized paradoxical reaction. Adding it 3 days before the ketamine restart muddies the antidepressant decision and confounds the ketamine attribution. The MIRAGE trial supports mirtazapine 7.5 mg for elderly insomnia generally, but Dad's specific prior reaction is decisive. Defer until the antidepressant strategy is settled (see handout #09).
The 5/18 IV ketamine restart is itself a sleep intervention. In TRD responders — and Dad's February response qualifies him — sleep improvement appears within 24 hours of the first IV session, and the early-morning-waking pattern specifically tends to resolve within the first 1–2 sessions. The Rodrigues 2022 study found that improvement in early-morning waking was a significant mediator of ketamine's antidepressant effect. Watch for it. Restarting Ativan, even at 0.5 mg HS. Would undo the gains of the recently completed taper. The same reasoning that puts klonopin off the table applies more directly here. The current baseline of being off all benzodiazepines is worth preserving through the bridge.
Strength of evidence for this bridge strategy: ★★★★
The "do" recommendations (trazodone uptitration + melatonin titration) have moderate evidence individually; their combined effect for a 2–3 night bridge in Dad's exact profile isn't directly trialed. The "don't" recommendations are anchored in strong guidelines (AGS Beers, AASM RBD), the recently completed Ativan taper, and unambiguous mechanism (anticholinergic burden given the pending neurology workup). The asymmetry is intentional: the strongest evidence is for what to avoid, and the "do" side is a defensible default within that constraint.
A defensible alternative: standing pat
  • The "no changes" interpretation is also defensible. Doxepin 6 mg has only been at therapeutic dose for ~10 days, and sleep-maintenance response to doxepin continues to build over 2–4 weeks. The partial benefit Dad reports may still be improving. If the family prefers to leave the stack entirely unchanged for the next 3 nights — keep trazodone 50 mg PRN as before, leave melatonin at 3 mg — that's a reasonable choice too. The uptitrations above are a modest optimization, not a necessity.
  • The ketamine restart is the main event. Anything done in the next 72 hours is a bridge, not a treatment. The IV restart on 5/18 is the intervention that's likely to move the picture — the 2–3 nights before it should be managed conservatively, not optimized aggressively.
  • Behavioral and environmental factors deserve as much attention as pharmacology. Consistent wake time, morning light, room temperature, and not catastrophizing about each night's sleep all matter materially. A 76-year-old's sleep architecture responds to behavioral discipline at least as readily as to medication adjustments — especially in the short window of a 3-night bridge.
  • Dad's instinct about antihistamines is mostly right — but doxepin 6 mg is the right kind of antihistamine. Most OTC sleep aids (ZZZquil, Benadryl, Unisom) are antihistamines, and Dad is right to be skeptical of them. Doxepin at 3–6 mg is also an antihistamine — but a highly H1-selective one with negligible anticholinergic burden, ~775× more potent at H1 than diphenhydramine. The difference between the right kind and the wrong kind matters more than the category.

Tier 1 — Major Clinical Guidelines ★★★★★
Arnold MJ. Beers Criteria for Inappropriate Medication Use in Older Adults: Update From the American Geriatrics Society.
Am Fam Physician 2024;109:374–375. Read here.
Key findingThe 2023 AGS Beers Criteria explicitly list first-generation antihistamines (diphenhydramine, doxylamine, hydroxyzine) as "avoid" in older adults due to strong anticholinergic properties. Benzodiazepines (including clonazepam) and Z-drugs (zolpidem, eszopiclone) are also "avoid" categories due to fall and cognitive risks. Doxepin > 6 mg/day is flagged as having strong anticholinergic properties; doxepin ≤ 6 mg/day is acceptable.
How it appliesThe authoritative geriatric prescribing reference. Anchors the rejection of ZZZquil/Benadryl/Unisom, hydroxyzine, klonopin, and Z-drugs. Also anchors the "don't titrate doxepin above 6 mg" recommendation.
Howell M, Avidan AY et al. Management of REM Sleep Behavior Disorder: AASM Clinical Practice Guideline.
J Clin Sleep Med 2023;19:759–768. Read here.
Key findingConditional recommendation for immediate-release melatonin (starting 3 mg, titrating in 3 mg increments up to 15 mg) for isolated and secondary RBD. Notes that melatonin is preferred over clonazepam in older patients and those with neurodegenerative disease because it is "only mildly sedating" with reduced risk of confusion, morning drowsiness, and falls.
How it appliesThe melatonin uptitration recommendation is directly anchored here. Also anchors the broader rejection of clonazepam in Dad's profile — the dedicated RBD guideline itself recommends melatonin over clonazepam in his exact age and disease context.
Morin CM, Buysse DJ. Management of Insomnia.
NEJM 2024;391:247–258. Read here.
Key findingCurrent NEJM clinical review of insomnia. Identifies low-dose doxepin (3–6 mg) and orexin antagonists as the two pharmacologic classes with the most favorable safety profile in older adults. Z-drugs and benzodiazepines carry meaningful fall and cognitive risks. Recommends CBT-I as first-line treatment with pharmacotherapy as augmentation.
How it appliesSupports continuing doxepin 6 mg as the anchor and confirms that the alternatives the family is being asked about (ZZZquil, klonopin, Ambien) are not in the recommended set for older adults with Dad's profile.
Tier 2 — Major RCTs and Mechanism ★★★★
Scharf M, Rogowski R et al. Efficacy and Safety of Doxepin 1 mg, 3 mg, and 6 mg in Elderly Patients With Primary Insomnia.
J Clin Psychiatry 2008;69:1557–1564. Read here.
Key findingRandomized double-blind placebo-controlled crossover trial in adults ≥65 with primary insomnia. Doxepin at 1, 3, and 6 mg all produced dose-related improvements in wake time after sleep onset, total sleep time, and sleep efficiency — including during the final third of the night (the early-morning awakening period). Side-effect profile was comparable to placebo.
How it appliesThe trial establishing efficacy of low-dose doxepin for the exact sleep pattern Dad has (early-morning awakening). Confirms 6 mg as the right anchor dose; the lack of meaningful side-effect signal up to 6 mg supports the dose ceiling.
Maruani J, Reynaud E et al. Efficacy of Melatonin and Ramelteon for the Acute and Long-Term Management of Insomnia Disorder in Adults: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis.
J Sleep Res 2023;32:e13939. Read here.
Key findingMeta-analysis of melatonin and ramelteon trials. Melatonin produces modest but consistent improvements in sleep latency (~7 minute reduction) and total sleep time (~8 minute increase) vs. placebo. Safety profile is excellent. Effect size is largest in older adults, where endogenous melatonin decline is more pronounced.
How it appliesThe melatonin uptitration from 3 → 5 mg is anchored in this evidence base — modest individual effect but favorable safety profile, and the additive effect on doxepin is mechanistically rational (different receptor systems).
Tier 3 — Systematic Reviews and Patient-Specific Context ★★★
Rodrigues NB et al. Do Sleep Changes Mediate the Anti-Depressive and Anti-Suicidal Response of Intravenous Ketamine in Treatment-Resistant Depression?
J Sleep Res 2022;31:e13400. Read here.
Key findingStudy of 323 patients with TRD receiving IV ketamine. Improvement in early-morning waking was a significant mediator of both the antidepressant and anti-suicidal effects of ketamine — each one-point improvement in total sleep score was associated with ~3.3× higher odds of treatment response.
How it appliesThe 5/18 IV ketamine restart is expected to specifically address Dad's 4 AM awakening pattern. This is a major reason not to add a new sleep agent during the bridge — the ketamine itself is the most likely intervention to actually move the sleep picture.
Albert SM, Roth T et al. Sleep Health and Appropriate Use of OTC Sleep Aids in Older Adults — Recommendations of a Gerontological Society of America Workgroup.
The Gerontologist 2017;57:163–170. Read here.
Key findingOver 50% of older adults using OTC sleep aids are taking diphenhydramine or doxylamine, and most are unaware of the safety risks. The Gerontological Society of America workgroup explicitly recommends against these products in older adults due to anticholinergic burden, fall risk, and cognitive concerns. ZZZquil and Unisom SleepTabs are the most common OTC offenders.
How it appliesDirectly addresses Dad's question about ZZZquil. The geriatric specialty society guidance is unambiguous — these products are inappropriate for his age and profile, with the additional concerns of his possible Lewy prodrome and brain fog amplifying the standard caution.
Pottie K, Thompson W et al. Deprescribing Benzodiazepine Receptor Agonists: Evidence-Based Clinical Practice Guideline.
Can Fam Physician 2018;64:339–351. Read here.
Key findingThe Bruyère Research Institute clinical practice guideline for tapering benzodiazepine receptor agonists in adults ≥65. Recommends gradual taper after >4 weeks of use and emphasizes consolidating taper gains. Notes that after a successful taper, restarting a benzodiazepine — particularly a longer-acting agent — typically produces a harder taper trajectory the second time, which is why preserving completed tapers is preferred over restart-and-retaper cycles.
How it appliesThe reasoning for keeping klonopin and other benzodiazepines off the table for the bridge. The recently completed Ativan taper is worth protecting; restarting a benzodiazepine — even a longer-acting one — would mean repeating that taper work later with a harder trajectory.
