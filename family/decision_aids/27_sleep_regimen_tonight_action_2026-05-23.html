Why this regimen, not the others

Why lemborexant 10 mg single dose (not split)

Last night's lemborexant 10 mg total taken as half-tablet (5 mg) + later half-tablet (5 mg) was pharmacokinetically suboptimal. Lemborexant has a half-life of ~18 hours and is designed to be taken as a single dose immediately before bedtime; the FDA label specifies single-dose administration. The first 5 mg partially cleared before the second 5 mg achieved peak; neither dose alone reached the receptor occupancy of a single 10 mg bolus. Lemborexant has not been adequately trialed yet — last night's failure shouldn't be read as the drug failing for Dad. Lemborexant carries the lowest fall risk (no postural-stability impairment in elderly studies) and the cleanest pre-ECT profile of the options.

Why melatonin 5 mg rather than 10 mg

Evidence for melatonin benefit above 5 mg in elderly is absent; doses above 5 mg are supraphysiologic with no demonstrated additional sleep benefit. Maintaining the 10 mg dose adds no advantage and may produce daytime grogginess.

Why lorazepam at bedside as rescue, not at start of night

Combining lemborexant + lorazepam at the start of the night risks (a) muddying the assessment of whether lemborexant works for Dad; (b) additive sedation/fall risk; (c) reinforcing a sleep-requires-Ativan pattern. Holding 0.5 mg in reserve as a rescue preserves the option without committing to the combination. One dose if truly needed at 90 minutes of non-sleep is appropriate; a second dose mid-night is not.

The initial OE #26 (5/23 morning) ranked lemborexant Tier 1 and quetiapine Tier 4, driven by population-level chronic-use safety signals (Hui 2025: HR 3.1 mortality / HR 8.1 dementia / HR 2.8 falls vs trazodone; Højlund 2022: aHR 1.52 MACE). The "Option B is contraindicated" framing in the initial OE was over-calibrated for Dad's specific case.

Sequencing and failure criteria

If Option A (lemborexant) is chosen — commit to 2–3 nights as a fair trial

Pharmacological rationale for sticking with lemborexant for 2–3 nights: lemborexant has a 17–19 hour half-life and accumulates with daily dosing; plasma levels on Night 2 are ~50% higher than Night 1, and by Night 3–4 you're approaching steady state. Combined with: partial sleep gain on Night 1 (even if modest) reducing homeostatic pressure for Night 2; reduced "is this going to work" anxiety after some sleep is achieved; and the OE #23 medium-term-sleep-regimen finding that lemborexant requires a 1–2 week trial to assess. Switching after one suboptimal night doesn't give the agent a fair pharmacokinetic chance.

Concrete criteria for Night 2 decision if Option A chosen:

  • If Night 1 sleep ≥4 hours: retry same regimen Night 2 — accumulation effect expected to help.
  • If Night 1 sleep 2–4 hours: retry same regimen Night 2 (fair-trial principle) but consider adding lorazepam 0.5 mg HS as a scheduled adjunct (not just PRN) to provide GABAergic floor while the orexin blockade builds up.
  • If Night 1 sleep <2 hours (essentially zero): switch to Option B (quetiapine 25 mg HS) for Night 2 — at that point the cumulative sleep crisis is dangerous enough that the known-tolerated agent's floor wins over the fair-trial principle.
  • If Night 1 sleep ≥6 hours: working regimen, hold steady; reassess after 3–4 nights.
If Option B (quetiapine 25 mg) is chosen — Night 2 decision is more dose-titration than agent-switch

Concrete criteria for Night 2 decision if Option B chosen:

  • If Night 1 sleep ≥6 hours: hold at 25 mg HS.
  • If Night 1 sleep 3–5 hours: escalate to 50 mg HS for Night 2 (defensible based on the 11/2025 prior tolerance at 50 mg × 4 weeks).
  • If Night 1 sleep <3 hours: something is meaningfully different from the 11/2025 trial. Consider switching to Option A (lemborexant 10 mg single dose) for Night 2, with explicit framing that the prior split-dose was the failure mode.
