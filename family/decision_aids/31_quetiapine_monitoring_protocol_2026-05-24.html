1. Baseline workup — order this week (serves both quetiapine monitoring AND pre-ECT)

Tests to order this week from PCP Dr. Straka or via U-M intake
  • 12-lead EKG. Highest priority. Dad's 5/19/2025 EKG (LAFB, HR 49, PR 214, QRS 120, QTc 401) is >1 year old. Required by FDA quetiapine label for patients with bradycardia / conduction abnormalities; required for pre-ECT anesthesia clearance. ★★★★★
  • CMP including potassium and magnesium. Hypokalemia + hypomagnesemia are major risk factors for severe QTc prolongation (Wang 2024). Also required pre-ECT. ★★★★★
  • CBC. Required pre-ECT; modest yield for quetiapine baseline. ★★★★★
  • TSH (and free T4 if TSH abnormal). Quetiapine FDA label notes thyroid effects. Recent values may suffice. ★★★★★
  • Fasting glucose or HbA1c. Baseline metabolic; recent value within 3 months may suffice. ★★★★★
  • Weight measurement. Document baseline. Recent CVS records show 176 lbs at 12/2024 + unintentional weight loss noted. ★★★★★
  • Orthostatic vital signs in office (supine → standing BP/HR at 1 + 3 min). ★★★★★
  • Brief cognitive baseline — Mini-Cog (3-item recall + clock draw, ~3 min) or MoCA if PCP capable. Useful both for quetiapine monitoring AND as the pre-ECT cognitive baseline (ECT cognitive monitoring is standard). ★★★★★

NOT needed for the bridge: echocardiogram (defer to U-M anesthesia decision); stress test (not indicated for asymptomatic stable bradycardia per ACC/AHA 2018 — Class III); Holter monitor (not indicated for asymptomatic stable conduction findings); repeat fasting lipids (already on rosuvastatin with recent values).

2. Daily home monitoring — orthostatic vitals

Orthostatic vital sign protocol

Why daily for first 3 days: quetiapine's α₁-adrenergic blockade produces orthostatic hypotension in 6–18% of elderly users (El-Saifi 2016). Dad's baseline BP 112/58 is low-normal — already orthostatically vulnerable. The 5/24 morning was reassuring (no orthostasis observed), but day-to-day variability is real.

How to measure (per JAMA Internal Medicine 2026 / Moloney guidance):

  • Sit Dad quietly for 3–5 minutes; measure BP/HR seated
  • Stand him up; wait 1 minute; measure BP/HR standing
  • Wait another 2 minutes (3 minutes total standing); measure again
  • Automated home BP cuff is adequate (no special equipment needed)
  • Note any symptoms during the standing phase (dizziness, lightheadedness, visual dimming, palpitations)

Schedule:

  • Days 1–3 (5/24, 5/25, 5/26): Each morning before first activity AND 30–60 minutes after any HS quetiapine dose taken at unusual time
  • Days 4–7: Once daily morning measurement if no drops detected
  • Week 2 onward: Every other day or symptom-triggered

Thresholds for action:

  • Symptomatic orthostatic event (dizziness, lightheadedness, visual dimming on standing): contact Dr. L / Dr. Rubin same day regardless of measured drop
  • Asymptomatic SBP drop ≥20 mmHg or DBP drop ≥10 mmHg within 3 minutes = defined orthostatic hypotension. Document; report at next clinical contact
  • Asymptomatic standing SBP <90 mmHg: dose reduction discussion warranted
  • Any near-syncope or syncope → immediate medical evaluation

3. Daily home monitoring — fall risk and ambulation

Fall-precaution protocol — given baseline gait abnormality + balance + neuropathy

Hui 2025 showed HR 2.8 for falls with low-dose quetiapine vs. trazodone in chronic use. Dad's baseline already includes gait abnormality (R26.9), balance problem (R26.89), and peripheral neuropathy (G62.9). The OE #28 / handout #28 fall-precaution framework applies fully here.

  • Sit-dangle-stand sequence for every bed-to-bathroom transfer, especially overnight: sit on edge of bed for 30–60 seconds, assess for dizziness, then stand holding stable surface for 10–15 seconds before walking
  • Environment now: nightlight on path to bathroom; non-slip mats; remove throw rugs; bedside urinal for nighttime use to minimize nocturnal ambulation
  • Family log of falls and near-falls. A "near-fall" is a stumble, grabbing furniture for support, or any episode where Dad reported needing to catch himself. Both falls and near-falls should be logged
  • Walker consideration: introduce if any fall occurs, or if ≥2 near-falls during the bridge, or if family observes new gait unsteadiness beyond baseline
  • Bedside commode: reasonable proactive measure given the nocturnal sedation + low BP + gait + neuropathy combination. Worth requesting from PCP this week

Discontinuation thresholds:

  • Any fall WITH injury (fracture, head strike, laceration requiring care) → immediate quetiapine discontinuation discussion with Dr. L / Dr. Rubin
  • Two or more falls (even without injury) during the bridge → reassessment of risk-benefit; likely dose reduction or change of agent

4. Daily family observation — Lewy-spectrum sensitivity (highest-vigilance domain)

Why this matters most

Severe neuroleptic sensitivity reactions can occur in up to 50% of patients with established Lewy body dementia (DLB Consortium, Kaila 2025). Dad has possible prodromal Lewy-spectrum disease (not established DLB), and he tolerated quetiapine 50 mg × 4 weeks in 11/2025 without EPS — meaningful prior-tolerance signal. But the substrate can progress, and sensitivity may emerge. The DLB Consortium's standing recommendation: warn both patient and caregiver of the possibility of a severe sensitivity reaction before any antipsychotic trial. Quetiapine has the lowest D2 affinity of atypicals and is the preferred antipsychotic in DLB — but is not zero-risk.

Daily checklist — observe and log if any are new or worsened
  • New or worsened rest tremor — especially hands or jaw, when sitting still
  • Stiffness or rigidity — difficulty turning in bed, reduced arm swing while walking, "wooden" quality of movement
  • Bradykinesia — slower dressing, slower eating, slower walking than baseline
  • Postural instability — new tendency to fall backward, retropulsion
  • Shuffling gait or freezing — feet stuck to floor, small steps
  • Facial masking — reduced facial expressiveness compared to recent baseline
  • Cognitive fluctuations — episodes of staring, marked inattention, or confusion that alternate with periods of near-normal function (this is the hallmark of DLB cognitive symptoms)
  • Visual hallucinations or illusions — seeing people, animals, or shapes that aren't there; misidentifying objects (cardinal DLB feature)
  • RBD worsening — more frequent dream enactment, more violent movements, vocalizations, falls from bed
  • Dysphagia — any new difficulty swallowing

If any one of these is new or markedly worsened: contact Dr. L / Dr. Rubin within 24 hours.

ACUTE NEUROLEPTIC SENSITIVITY REACTION — immediate discontinuation + urgent medical evaluation

If ANY of the following emerge, stop quetiapine immediately and seek urgent medical evaluation:

  • Sudden severe rigidity
  • High fever + rigidity (possible NMS — call 911)
  • Marked new cognitive decline (sudden confusion, unable to recognize family)
  • Severe sedation not clearing by mid-morning (unable to be roused)
  • Autonomic instability (wide BP swings, marked tachycardia, profuse diaphoresis)
  • New dysphagia with risk of aspiration

5. Sedation, cognitive, and ADL monitoring

Pragmatic family-administrable assessments
  • Next-day sedation assessment. Family notes time Dad is "fully alert and functional" each morning. Threshold: if grogginess persists past ~10 AM (assuming ~7 AM wake), warrants dose-reduction discussion. (No published validated threshold specific to quetiapine — this is expert-consensus calibrated.)
  • Weekly Mini-Cog (3-item recall + clock draw, ~3 minutes). Recommended by AGS for cognitive screening in older adults. Administer at baseline (this week with Dr. Straka or self-administered) and weekly during the bridge. Score <3 / 5 OR clock-draw abnormal = concerning; report to Dr. L.
  • ADL daily check. Simple observed checklist: dressing independently, eating, toileting, grooming, ambulating without assistance. Any new dependence or marked slowness should be logged.
  • Epworth Sleepiness Scale (ESS) weekly. Validated 8-item self-report for daytime sleepiness. Score >10 suggests excessive daytime sleepiness; report at next clinical contact.
  • PHQ-9 weekly. Severe depression at baseline (24); track for any change. Worsening would inform ECT acceleration urgency.

6. Sleep monitoring during the bridge

Sleep diary — standard clinical tool, strongly recommended

Simple daily log capturing: bedtime, estimated sleep onset, number and duration of awakenings, final wake time, total estimated sleep time, daytime naps, any new dream content or RBD-like episodes. This data will be valuable for the U-M ECT team. ★★★★★ (standard sleep medicine practice).

Consumer wearables (Oura, Fitbit, Apple Watch): reasonable accuracy for total sleep time and wake detection in healthy adults (~85% Oura/Fitbit), but significant individual-level inaccuracies and not validated in RBD or possible-DLB populations. May provide supplementary trend data but should not replace the sleep diary or drive clinical decisions. ★★★★★ for this population.

Dose adjustment triggers based on sleep data:

  • Total sleep time <5 hours for 2+ consecutive nights → escalate to 50 mg HS as single dose (defensible from prior tolerance) OR continue split-dose but add midnight 25 mg at first awakening
  • Total sleep time consistently >9 hours with morning grogginess past noon → reduce to 25 mg HS only (no midnight redose); reassess after 2 nights
  • New nightmares or dream enactment → log; report at next clinical contact; consider RBD-targeted adjustment with U-M

7. Red flags — immediate action required

CALL PRESCRIBER OR 911 PER SEVERITY
  1. Syncope or near-syncope (loss of consciousness, even momentary) → 911 if loss of consciousness or fall with head strike; otherwise urgent prescriber contact
  2. Any fall with injury → hold quetiapine, evaluate fracture / head injury; reassess risk-benefit with Dr. L / Dr. Rubin
  3. Chest pain, palpitations, or new irregular pulse → hold quetiapine, obtain EKG urgently
  4. Symptomatic bradycardia (HR <45 with dizziness, fatigue, exertional dyspnea) → hold quetiapine, urgent evaluation
  5. Severe sedation not clearing by mid-morning (cannot be roused, persistently confused or somnolent past 10 AM) → hold next dose, contact prescriber
  6. Any new motor symptoms (new rigidity, marked tremor increase, dyskinesia, severe gait deterioration, dysphagia) → immediately discontinue quetiapine, urgent evaluation for neuroleptic sensitivity reaction
  7. New cognitive symptoms (acute confusion, visual hallucinations, delusions, failure to recognize family) → immediately discontinue quetiapine, urgent evaluation
  8. NMS pattern (high fever + rigidity + autonomic instability + altered mental status) → discontinue quetiapine, call 911
  9. Significant unexpected weight gain (>5 lbs in a week) → evaluate for fluid retention rather than metabolic effect; contact prescriber

9. What to escalate at the Dr. L → Dr. Maxner U-M ECT call

Monitoring-specific agenda items beyond ECT scheduling acceleration
  • Bridge agent preference. Does U-M ECT prefer quetiapine to continue as the sleep bridge, or would they prefer a different agent? Some ECT programs have preferences regarding concurrent antipsychotics (quetiapine has modest anticonvulsant properties potentially affecting seizure threshold)
  • Pre-ECT labs. Will the EKG + CMP + CBC + TSH ordered this week serve as the pre-ECT workup, or does U-M require their own? Most anesthesia departments accept labs within 30 days for low-risk procedures
  • Cardiac substrate communication. Explicit communication of LAFB + HR 49 + PR 214 + QRS 120. Does U-M anesthesia want a cardiology consult or echocardiogram pre-ECT? (Per APA resource document, bradyarrhythmias with hypotension can elevate ECT risk)
  • Quetiapine timing relative to ECT. Should quetiapine continue through ECT (many programs continue low-dose), taper before first session, or hold on treatment mornings only? At 25–50 mg HS, abrupt discontinuation is generally safe without taper
  • Cognitive baseline preference. Does U-M want a specific cognitive instrument (MoCA, MMSE) administered pre-ECT for comparison during the course?
  • Lewy-spectrum context. Communicate the pending DAT-SPECT and U-M Movement Disorders workup. ECT has case-report evidence of antidepressive and motor benefits in Lewy body disease, but robust evidence is sparse. The ECT team should be aware of the diagnostic consideration
  • Cymbalta through ECT. Confirm hold at 60 mg per PRIDE study + Pluijms 2021 meta-analysis. Defer any taper decision to post-ECT
  • Offer to forward monitoring data: sleep diary, orthostatic log, EKG/lab results to ECT team for their records
