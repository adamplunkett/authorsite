Should Dad take Lamictal instead of Depakote?
Decision Aid · 2026-05-12
For Dad, Mom, Dr. Rubin, the K Clinic
psychiatrist, Julane & the family team
Recommendation

Lamictal is meaningfully safer than Depakote in Dad's specific profile on the dimensions that matter most (movement, brain volume, B12-axis). But Dr. Rubin's Depakote is being prescribed specifically for anxiety — and for anxiety in older adults with Dad's neurological profile, neither drug has strong evidence, and several limitations apply equally to both. The most evidence-supported step is deferring both drugs until after Dad's ketamine response has been assessed at 2–3 sessions, with the K Clinic psychiatrist consulted on what anxiety treatments are appropriate for Dad's specific profile.

Why this matters for Dad specifically

Dad's profile makes the comparison between Depakote and Lamictal genuinely consequential: he has formal REM Sleep Behavior Disorder on his chart since 2017 (8.5 years), prior Parkinson's-like side effects from low-dose aripiprazole in 2025, self-discontinued brexpiprazole for balance issues in March 2026, balance impairment coded 9 months before any recent medication changes, "somewhat robotic" gait noted on his April 14 PCP exam, brain fog coded multiple times in April and May, just-diagnosed methylmalonic acidemia with active B12 supplementation, and an active neurology referral specifically to rule out Parkinson's. Depakote's documented risks (movement side effects, brain shrinkage in cognitively vulnerable older adults, B12-axis disruption) align with these vulnerabilities; Lamictal does not. The indication being targeted (anxiety, per Mom's clarification) further weakens the case for either drug, because evidence for both in late-life anxiety is essentially absent — and better-evidenced anxiety treatments exist for older adults with this kind of neurological profile.

The evidence
How Lamictal is safer than Depakote in Dad's profile Lamictal-specific concerns to weigh
No insidious Parkinson's-like syndrome. Depakote has a well-documented pattern of causing slowly developing Parkinson's-like symptoms (tremor, stiffness, slowed movement) — often combined with subtle cognitive decline that can mimic dementia. Lamictal does not have this profile. FDA-labeled warning for worsening Parkinson's symptoms in patients with pre-existing PD. A 1997 trial (Shinotoh et al.) gave Lamictal to 12 patients with Parkinson's disease; 9 didn't finish the study because of side effects, including 3 with hallucinations and 4 with worsening of Parkinson's-like symptoms. Dad doesn't have a Parkinson's diagnosis, but the active neurology referral exists precisely to evaluate whether something like that is present — making this signal worth taking seriously.
No brain-shrinkage signal. The 2011 trial (Tariot et al.) that found accelerated brain volume loss in older adults given roughly the same Depakote dose Dad is prescribed has no equivalent for Lamictal. Lamictal is generally regarded as "cognitively friendly" in older adults. Lamictal increases the amount of time spent in REM sleep. Because Dad has formally diagnosed REM Sleep Behavior Disorder (a condition where the muscle paralysis that normally accompanies REM sleep is incomplete, so people act out their dreams), more REM time could theoretically mean a longer window for dream-enactment episodes. A single case report described a patient on Lamictal whose RBD didn't worsen during treatment but flared when the drug was abruptly stopped — so the picture is mixed, not clearly harmful.
Doesn't disrupt the B12 / folate axis. Dad's August 6 blood test (to check whether his B12 supplementation is working) would not be confounded by Lamictal the way it would by Depakote. Theoretical concern about blunting ketamine, but real-world data is reassuring. Lamictal reduces release of a brain chemical called glutamate, and ketamine works partly by causing a brief glutamate surge — so in theory they could work against each other. Brain imaging in healthy volunteers supports this concern in principle. But the strongest real-world clinical data has not shown it play out: a 2025 Yale study of 347 patients (57 on Lamictal during ketamine treatment) found no difference in depression outcomes; a smaller 2023 Mayo Clinic study found the same. Caveat: both studies used intravenous or intranasal ketamine — Dad's planned treatment is sublingual (under the tongue), which has not been studied with Lamictal.
Doesn't muddy the neurology evaluation. Lamictal does not produce the same Parkinson's-like side effect pattern that would confuse Dad's June neurology evaluation the way Depakote would. Rare but serious skin reaction. Lamictal can cause Stevens-Johnson syndrome, a life-threatening skin reaction, in about 1 in 2,500 patients — which is why it requires a mandatory slow start over 5–6 weeks. The risk is highest in the first month and increases if the drug is started too quickly.
Better survival in patients with both epilepsy and dementia. A 2026 Swedish population study of nearly 6,000 patients found that those given Lamictal for seizures had lower death rates, while those given Depakote had the highest death rates of any seizure medication studied. Dad does not have dementia, but the data are relevant if a Parkinson's-spectrum diagnosis emerges. Common side effects that could compound existing issues. Dizziness, balance problems, and headache are documented side effects of Lamictal in older adults. In a patient with already-documented balance impairment and gait findings, these are worth watching.
What's true of both drugs (shared limitations)
  • Neither drug can deliver short-term anxiety relief. Lamictal requires a mandatory 5–6 week slow start (25 mg/day for 2 weeks, then 50 mg/day for 2 more, then 100 mg/day or higher) to prevent the Stevens-Johnson skin reaction, and studies in depression typically measured benefit at 8–10 weeks at full dose. Depakote at the prescribed 250 mg/day is below the dose at which any benefit has been documented for any approved use (typical doses are 750–2,500 mg/day), and even at therapeutic doses takes 4–8 weeks to act. Both drugs are essentially medium-term medications, not short-term agitation treatments.
  • Neither drug has been tested in older adults with hard-to-treat depression or anxiety. No randomized placebo-controlled trial has tested either drug in adults over 65 with these conditions, at any dose. The landmark trial of add-on treatments in older adults with hard-to-treat depression (OPTIMUM, NEJM 2023) included aripiprazole, bupropion, lithium, and nortriptyline — but neither Depakote nor Lamictal.
  • Neither drug is recommended by major clinical guidelines as a first-line add-on for hard-to-treat depression. The American Psychiatric Association, Canada's CANMAT, the UK's NICE, the Maudsley Hospital prescribing guide, the geriatric psychiatry association (AAGP), and the U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs all omit both drugs from their first-line recommendations. (Lamictal sometimes appears as "at best second-line"; Depakote is absent entirely.)
  • For anxiety specifically — which is the actual indication being targeted — the evidence for both is even thinner. Depakote has a single small trial in young-to-middle-aged men with generalized anxiety (never replicated), and a 2024 meta-analysis of valproate's anxiety effects across all studies was not statistically significant. Lamictal has essentially no published evidence supporting use for anxiety. Any continued anxiety treatment in Dad's specific profile (formal RBD, possible prodromal Parkinson's-spectrum features, MMA-confirmed functional B12 deficiency, autonomic findings) should be discussed with the K Clinic psychiatrist with attention to his particular neurological vulnerabilities — not just to the anxiety indication alone.
Strength of evidence supporting this recommendation: ★★★★★
Lamictal's safety advantage over Depakote on movement, brain-volume, and B12-axis dimensions is well-established. Both drugs lacking strong evidence in older adults for either depression or anxiety is also well-established. The combination supports the conclusion that Lamictal is the safer of the two if a mood stabilizer is used at all, but that neither drug is well-matched to the anxiety indication being targeted, and that better-evidenced anxiety options exist.

  2.   3. Hold Depakote at the current 250 mg/day dose pending review. Do not allow titration to a higher dose.
  4. Proceed with sublingual ketamine restart May 18 as planned. Assess response over 2–3 sessions before introducing additional medications.
  5. Continue B12 1000 mcg daily. Protect the August 6 MMA recheck from confounders.
  6. If new symptoms emerge on either drug — movement changes, sedation, cognitive shift, rash, dizziness — stop and notify Dr. Rubin and the neurology team.
Bottom line Lamictal is meaningfully safer than Depakote in Dad's profile on movement, brain-volume, and B12-axis dimensions. But the indication being targeted is anxiety, and neither drug has strong evidence for treating anxiety in older adults — and several shared limitations make both poor matches for what's currently being asked. The honest answer to "is Lamictal any better?" is yes on safety vs. Depakote, but neither drug is the right tool for this job. The most evidence-supported step is deferring both, asking the K Clinic psychiatrist about anxiety treatments appropriate for an older adult with Dad's specific neurological profile, and letting ketamine response and the neurology evaluation play out before any new mood-stabilizer decision.
Key Studies & Findings
Ranked by authority · Each citation summarized for how it applies to Dad's case
Tier 1 — Major Clinical Guidelines ★★★★★
Steffens DC. Treatment-Resistant Depression in Older Adults.
NEJM 2024;390:630–639. Read here.
Key findingThe current NEJM review of treatment-resistant depression in adults ≥65 lists aripiprazole, bupropion, lithium, triiodothyronine, nortriptyline, MAOIs, esketamine, ECT, and TMS as recommended next-step strategies. Lamictal is not mentioned.
How it appliesThe most current authoritative summary of late-life treatment-resistant depression does not endorse Lamictal as an add-on treatment — though notably it also doesn't endorse Depakote, which underscores that neither drug is in the recommended first-line set.
Taylor RW et al. Pharmacological Augmentation in Unipolar Depression: A Guide to the Guidelines.
Int J Neuropsychopharmacol 2020;23:587–625. Read here.
Key findingSystematic review of 10 major depression guidelines. Lithium and atypical antipsychotics (like aripiprazole) were recommended by all 10. Lamictal was not universally recommended by any of the 10, though some guidelines mention it as a possible second-line option.
How it appliesAcross guideline frameworks worldwide, Lamictal has marginal but not consistent endorsement as an add-on treatment for treatment-resistant depression — better than Depakote's complete absence, but not first-line.
Tier 2 — Major RCTs ★★★★
Barbee JG et al. A Double-Blind Placebo-Controlled Trial of Lamotrigine as an Antidepressant Augmentation Agent in Treatment-Refractory Unipolar Depression.
J Clin Psychiatry 2011;72:1405–1412. Read here.
Key findingThe largest Western randomized placebo-controlled trial of Lamictal as add-on therapy for treatment-resistant depression. n=96. The primary endpoint was not met — Lamictal was not statistically better than placebo overall. Post-hoc analyses suggested some benefit in the most severely depressed patients.
How it appliesThe strongest Western evidence base for Lamictal in treatment-resistant depression failed its main test. This is a real evidence gap, even when comparing to Depakote (which has no equivalent placebo-controlled trial at all).
Shinotoh H et al. Lamotrigine Trial in Idiopathic Parkinsonism: A Double-Blind, Placebo-Controlled, Crossover Study.
Neurology 1997;48:1282–1285. Read here.
Key findingSmall double-blind crossover trial of Lamictal in 12 patients with idiopathic Parkinson's disease. 9 of 12 patients did not complete the study due to adverse effects: 2 with movement-related side effects (dyskinesia), 3 with hallucinations, and 4 with worsening of Parkinson's symptoms. The drug provided no symptomatic benefit.
How it appliesThis is the basis of the FDA-labeled signal for "exacerbation of Parkinsonian symptoms in patients with pre-existing Parkinson's disease." Dad does not have a confirmed Parkinson's diagnosis, but the active neurology referral exists precisely to evaluate whether something like that is present — so this trial's findings are directly relevant if Lamictal is being considered.
Lenze EJ et al. Antidepressant Augmentation versus Switch in Treatment-Resistant Geriatric Depression (OPTIMUM).
NEJM 2023;388:1067–1079. Read here.
Key findingThe landmark trial of add-on and switch strategies for treatment-resistant depression in adults ≥60. Tested aripiprazole, bupropion, lithium, and nortriptyline. Lamictal was not included as an option worth testing.
How it appliesThe most authoritative recent trial in the population most relevant to Dad did not consider Lamictal a candidate worth evaluating, suggesting it is not viewed as a leading option for this clinical situation.
Tier 3 — Meta-analyses & Systematic Reviews ★★★
Goh KK et al. Lamotrigine Augmentation in Treatment-Resistant Unipolar Depression: A Comprehensive Meta-Analysis.
J Psychopharmacol 2019;33:700–713. Read here.
Key findingMeta-analysis of 8 randomized trials (n=677). Overall pooled result favored Lamictal — but the positive effect was driven by six Chinese studies; the two non-Chinese RCTs were not statistically significant. The authors characterized Lamictal as "at best only second-line augmentation."
How it appliesThe most-cited meta-analysis supporting Lamictal in treatment-resistant depression has important limitations and explicitly does not recommend Lamictal as a first choice.
Arnone D et al. Efficacy of Lamotrigine in the Treatment of Unipolar and Bipolar Depression: Meta-Analysis.
Pharmaceuticals 2025;18:1590. Read here.
Key findingThe most recent meta-analysis, including previously unpublished trials. Concluded that Lamictal was not superior to placebo in unipolar depression — either as monotherapy or as an add-on.
How it appliesThe most up-to-date pooled evidence does not support Lamictal as an effective add-on for unipolar depression. This weakens the case for Lamictal even before considering Dad's specific profile.
Veraart JKE, Smith-Apeldoorn SY et al. Pharmacodynamic Interactions Between Ketamine and Psychiatric Medications.
Int J Neuropsychopharmacol 2021;24:808–831. Read here.
Key findingSystematic review of how psychiatric medications interact with ketamine. Concluded that benzodiazepines and "probably lamotrigine" reduce ketamine's antidepressant effect. The lamotrigine conclusion was based on 2 of 5 studies showing attenuation in research settings.
How it appliesThe theoretical concern about Lamictal blunting ketamine is real and reflected in the published systematic-review literature. This is the concern that real-world studies (below) have tested.
Santucci MC et al. Lamotrigine Is Not Associated With Differential Clinical Outcomes in a Real-World Sample of Patients With Unipolar Depression Receiving Ketamine or Esketamine.
Psychopharmacology 2025. Read here.
Key findingYale real-world study, n=347 patients receiving ketamine or esketamine for unipolar depression, including 57 patients on Lamictal at an average dose of 210 mg/day. Found no clinical evidence of attenuation: depression outcome scores (MADRS, QIDS) were the same in patients on Lamictal as in those not on Lamictal, even after adjusting for multiple confounders.
How it appliesThe strongest available real-world clinical evidence suggests the theoretical concern about Lamictal blunting ketamine does not translate to actual treatment outcomes — though this study used IV ketamine and intranasal esketamine, not the sublingual route Dad will be using.
Joseph B et al. Efficacy of Ketamine With and Without Lamotrigine in Treatment-Resistant Depression.
Pharmaceuticals 2023;16:1164. Read here.
Key findingSmaller Mayo Clinic preliminary report (n=13 on Lamictal). Found no significant differences in response or remission rates between patients receiving ketamine with vs. without Lamictal. A trend toward less dissociation (the brief altered-perception experience during ketamine sessions) with Lamictal, particularly with IV ketamine.
How it appliesA second real-world dataset corroborating the Yale finding, though with the same caveat: not sublingual ketamine.
Zelano J et al. Differences in Survival Associated With the First Antiseizure Medication in People With Dementia and Epilepsy.
Neurology 2026;106:e214929. Read here.
Key findingSwedish population-wide cohort study, n=5,764 patients with both epilepsy and dementia. Depakote was associated with the highest death rates (34% increased risk of dying compared to other seizure medications), while Lamictal was associated with the lowest death rates (16% reduced risk).
How it appliesDad does not have dementia, but if a Parkinson's-spectrum diagnosis emerges from the neurology evaluation, this is the most relevant comparative survival data on the two drugs in a cognitively vulnerable older population.
Postuma RB et al. Antidepressants and REM Sleep Behavior Disorder: Isolated Side Effect or Neurodegenerative Signal?
Sleep 2013;36:1579–1585. Read here.
Key findingProspective cohort study of 100 patients with REM Sleep Behavior Disorder confirmed by sleep study. Patients whose RBD was associated with antidepressant use had a substantially lower 5-year rate of progression to a Parkinson's-spectrum neurodegenerative diagnosis (22%) than patients whose RBD was unrelated to medications (59%).
How it appliesDad has been on antidepressants intermittently for years alongside his 8.5-year formal RBD diagnosis. This data suggests the antidepressant association may be partially protective against the highest published conversion rates — but his other findings (drug-induced parkinsonism, balance changes, robotic gait, brain fog) raise the concern back up substantially regardless of the antidepressant context.
Tier 4 — Mechanistic / Brain Imaging Studies ★★
Meiering MS et al. Functional Activity and Connectivity Signatures of Ketamine and Lamotrigine During Negative Emotional Processing.
Transl Psychiatry 2024;14:436. Read here.
Key findingBrain imaging study in healthy volunteers. Lamictal pretreatment blocked the brain connectivity changes produced by ketamine, and abolished the sustained (24-hour) effects on the brain network associated with depression response.
How it appliesThis is the strongest mechanistic evidence behind the theoretical concern that Lamictal could blunt ketamine's effect. It establishes that the interaction is real at the brain-imaging level, even though the clinical-outcome data above does not show it translating to depression outcomes.
Tier 5 — Smaller Studies & Tolerability Data ★
Aulakh JS et al. Tolerability and Effectiveness of Lamotrigine in Complex Elderly Patients.
J Geriatr Psychiatry Neurol 2005;18:8–11. Read here.
Key findingSmall retrospective case series (n=20) in elderly nursing home patients with agitated or aggressive behaviors. 19 of 20 tolerated Lamictal; 18 of 20 showed modest improvement. This was uncontrolled and hypothesis-generating.
How it appliesThe strongest available evidence specifically for Lamictal in elderly patients is a 20-patient uncontrolled study. This is illustrative of how thin the late-life evidence base is for Lamictal.
Economou NT et al. Lamotrigine Withdrawal May Worsen RBD Symptoms.
The Neurologist 2011;17:279–281. Read here.
Key findingSingle case report of a 68-year-old man with pre-existing REM Sleep Behavior Disorder who was started on Lamictal for suspected epilepsy. During 3 months of Lamictal treatment, there were no appreciable changes in RBD symptoms — but abrupt discontinuation of Lamictal severely worsened the RBD, with dreams becoming more vivid and frightening nearly nightly. Symptoms gradually subsided over 2 months after stopping.
How it appliesThis is the only published report directly addressing Lamictal in a patient with formally diagnosed RBD. It suggests Lamictal may have a modest stabilizing effect on RBD during treatment, but that abrupt withdrawal can cause significant rebound. The signal is weak (single case) and points in two directions.
