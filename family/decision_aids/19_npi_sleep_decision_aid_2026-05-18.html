Why this matters for Dad specifically

The activation-intolerance pattern across multiple psychiatric agents shifts the burden of sleep management toward behavioral and environmental interventions in a way it wouldn't for most patients. The recent overnight agitation episode (5/16–5/17) is a reminder that the pharmacologic levers are narrow, and that the family's ability to spot patterns and intervene early matters more than usual. The good news: the evidence base for non-pharmacologic sleep interventions in adults 70+ is actually strong — CBT-I has the largest effect size of any insomnia treatment in this age group, bright light therapy independently helps both sleep and depression, and the basic lifestyle adjustments (caffeine timing, alcohol limitation, exercise dose) have meta-analytic support specifically in older adults. The framing of this handout isn't "Dad needs to do all of this immediately" or "what's been done isn't enough" — Mom and Dad have already pulled multiple evidence-based interventions in (bright light therapy, biking, calming outdoor activities, separate sleep for RBD safety, audiobooks for rest). This handout layers on the next set of additions ranked by evidence and effort.

high yield
low effort		 Caffeine cutoff to noon (current: 3–4 PM; current intake 3–4 cups). The Gardiner 2023 meta-analysis recommended ≥8.8 hours pre-bed clearance for a standard cup of coffee — Dad's 3–4 PM cutoff gives ~6–8 hours, and elderly CYP1A2 metabolism is slower. Expected magnitude: ~20–45 minutes additional sleep + improved efficiency. Reducing total intake to 1–2 cups/day would compound the benefit. This is the single highest-yield lifestyle change.

Start digital CBT-I as the on-ramp while the in-person referral processes. Two options: Somryst (FDA-cleared, requires a prescription — ask Dr. R or Dr. Straka — typically 9 weeks, ~$899 list but often insurance-covered) or Sleepio (free in many regions via Big Health, 6 weeks). Digital CBT-I effect sizes are 0.21–1.09 across outcomes (roughly 60–80% of in-person effect on ISI in head-to-head studies — meaningful, not categorical). Important RBD adaptation: when the digital program reaches the "sleep restriction" component, hold the restriction conservative (floor at 5.5–6 hours rather than aggressive restriction) given the theoretical REM-rebound risk in RBD — or skip that one component until the in-person specialist can supervise it. The other components (stimulus control, cognitive restructuring, sleep hygiene, relaxation) don't need RBD-specific modification and produce most of the benefit anyway.

Confirm and optimize the bright light therapy already in use. If Dad is already doing morning bright light, the high-yield checks are: (1) intensity ≥10,000 lux at the eyes (lower-intensity boxes are common); (2) 30 minutes; (3) within ~30 minutes of waking. The 2025 JAMA Psychiatry meta-analysis (Menegaz de Almeida) showed 40.7% vs. 23.5% remission for non-seasonal depression — meaningful for sleep AND mood simultaneously. Morning biking outdoors gives the same lux exposure plus the exercise dose; combining them is additive.

Exercise bump from 2× to 3× per week, add a small resistance component. Per the Xiong 2025 network meta-analysis in older adults (62 RCTs), combined aerobic + resistance training was optimal at 660–990 METs·min/week with benefit from ~5 weeks — so starting earlier means crossing the benefit threshold earlier. Dad's biking 2× per week with Mom is already in the right zone; adding one more biking or walking day and a brief resistance set (bodyweight squats, wall push-ups, banded rows — 10–15 minutes, 2× per week) moves into the optimal dose. Morning timing aligns with the bright light exposure for additive circadian benefit. Cardiac caveats: LAFB and HR 49–53 are not contraindications to moderate exercise; gradual titration and orthostatic awareness are appropriate.

Start a 2-week sleep diary. Low cost, immediately useful for the DBSM and movement-disorders evaluations. Bedtime, wake time, awakenings, naps, caffeine/alcohol, any RBD episode. Paper or simple app. Foundational for separating ketamine effects, medication effects, and NPI effects over the coming weeks.

Nap management — short, early. ≤30 minutes, before 2 PM. The Souabni 2022 systematic review found brief early-afternoon naps don't impair nocturnal sleep in older adults; the Qin 2025 cohort found 30–90 minute naps associated with lower depression. Past 60 minutes or after 3 PM increases the risk of delaying sleep onset.

Finish the RBD bedroom-safety audit per AASM/IRBDSG. Not a sleep-quality intervention — an injury-prevention one. Dad and Mom are already doing the load-bearing piece (separate beds). Remaining items per the consensus (Howell 2023; During 2025): bedside soft mat / lower mattress, padded sharp edges, remove anything throwable that could injure, window protection if near. Quick walk-through this week.

Alcohol abstinence. Several converging lines of evidence make abstinence the indicated posture. Sleep and RBD: even ≤2 drinks suppress REM and fragment sleep (Gardiner 2025); AASM RBD guidance flags alcohol as a modifiable contributor to dream-enactment frequency via the REM-rebound mechanism. Medication interactions: duloxetine carries a hepatotoxicity warning with alcohol and increases bleeding risk; doxepin and trazodone produce additive CNS depression and TCA-class hepatotoxicity; tadalafil compounds hypotension on a baseline BP of 112/58; the just-completed lorazepam taper is at risk from a cross-tolerant depressant. Underlying conditions: alcohol is independently depressogenic; it raises falls risk on top of the existing gait/balance findings, LAFB, and bradycardia; it suppresses testosterone (already biochemically low); it worsens B12 status (relevant to the newly diagnosed methylmalonic acidemia). Dad's own N=1: per the clinical brief, his April 2024 voluntary-abstinence period coincided with the best functional state observed since early 2021.

Specialty referrals
initiate now		 In-lab polysomnography with REM Sleep Behavior Disorder montage. Highest-yield single diagnostic test in this picture. Coordinate with the U-M Movement Disorders evaluation already in motion. One study would simultaneously: (1) confirm and characterize the RBD with video-PSG (no formal PSG has been done since the 2017 G47.52 coding); (2) screen for obstructive sleep apnea, present in ~62% of MDD patients on PSG per Okada 2022 — and OSA can both mimic RBD and worsen depression/cognitive symptoms; (3) assess for periodic limb movement disorder; (4) provide baseline sleep architecture data; (5) inform whether clonazepam (a common RBD pharmacotherapy) is safe to consider — it's contraindicated in untreated OSA. This is arguably the single most important sleep recommendation in this handout.

Referral to Behavioral Sleep Medicine specialist (DBSM) for therapist-delivered CBT-I — running in parallel with the digital program above. Gold standard for chronic insomnia in adults 70+. NNT = 3 for the full in-person CBT-I package (Furukawa 2024); in-person delivery has iOR 1.83 (95% CI 1.19–2.81) vs. digital — meaningfully better, not categorically. Dad's profile — severe depression + comorbid RBD + multiple sleep disorders + activation-intolerance limiting pharma — places him squarely at level 3 of the AASM stepped-care model where individual therapist delivery is recommended. Key adaptation: sleep restriction needs to be implemented conservatively given the theoretical REM-rebound risk with RBD; the in-person specialist can supervise that component in a way a digital app can't. Lead time for appointment is the reason to refer now — the digital program above does most of the work in the meantime. Possible local options to ask Dr. R or Straka about: U-M Sleep Disorders Center, Henry Ford Sleep Lab.

Optional: 1–2 weeks of actigraphy for circadian-phase characterization. Older adults with lifetime depression typically show phase advancement (Hoyos 2020); confirming or ruling that out would inform whether morning light timing needs adjustment. Lower priority than PSG and CBT-I but cheap and informative if the sleep medicine workup is being done anyway.

Tier 1 — CBT-I in Older Adults and Depression ★★★★★
Furukawa Y, Sakata M, Yamamoto R et al. Components and Delivery Formats of Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Chronic Insomnia in Adults: A Systematic Review and Component Network Meta-Analysis.
JAMA Psychiatry 2024;81:357–365. Read here.
Key findingComponent network meta-analysis of 241 RCTs (31,452 participants). Full CBT-I package (cognitive restructuring + third-wave components + sleep restriction + stimulus control) delivered in-person had NNT = 3.0 (95% CI 2.3–4.3). In-person delivery was more efficacious than digital (iOR 1.83).
How it appliesThe single strongest evidence base for therapist-delivered CBT-I as the first-line non-pharmacologic insomnia intervention. The in-person delivery preference is why a DBSM referral matters more than a digital app for Dad's complex case.
Sadler P, McLaren S, Klein B, Harvey J, Jenkins M. Cognitive Behavior Therapy for Older Adults With Insomnia and Depression: A Randomized Controlled Trial.
Sleep 2018;41(8). Read here.
Key findingRCT in older adults (mean age 75 ± 7) with comorbid insomnia and depression. Both standard CBT-I and CBT-I plus positive-mood strategies produced significantly greater reductions in insomnia and depression severity vs. psychoeducation control, maintained at 20-week follow-up.
How it appliesThe age-matched and comorbidity-matched evidence that CBT-I is effective in patients exactly like Dad. The depression-comorbid context doesn't reduce the efficacy.

Key findingAASM clinical practice guideline. Bedroom safety good-practice statement: remove weapons, pad sharp edges, place soft mat next to bed, lower mattress, separate sleeping, pillow barrier if sharing bed, window protection. Conditional recommendation for melatonin and clonazepam as pharmacotherapy. Recommends formal video-PSG for diagnostic confirmation.
How it appliesThe AASM-endorsed source for the bedroom safety audit and for the PSG recommendation. Dad has had melatonin since RBD diagnosis (consistent with guideline); the safety audit completion and the formal PSG are the missing pieces.

Key findingInternational RBD Study Group consensus. Reinforces bedroom-safety measures regardless of current episode frequency (complex violent behaviors can occur unpredictably). Emphasizes systematic identification and modification of contributing factors (substances including alcohol).
How it appliesThe current international consensus that safety measures shouldn't be relaxed because episodes are infrequent, and that alcohol limitation is part of standard RBD management.

How it appliesThe empirical basis for prioritizing formal PSG in Dad's profile. Even without classic OSA symptoms, the prior probability is high enough to warrant the study — and the standard symptom-based screening misses cases.
Tier 3 — Bright Light Therapy in Late-Life Depression ★★★★
Menegaz de Almeida A, Aquino de Moraes FC, Cavalcanti Souza ME et al. Bright Light Therapy for Nonseasonal Depressive Disorders: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis.
JAMA Psychiatry 2025;82:38–46. Read here.
Key finding11 RCTs, 858 patients. Non-seasonal depression remission 40.7% on BLT vs. 23.5% on control (OR 2.42; 95% CI 1.50–3.91; p < 0.001). 10,000 lux × 30–60 min AM was the consistent dose.
How it appliesThe empirical basis for the bright light therapy add — meaningful effect on depression remission in non-seasonal MDD. If Dad is already doing this, the high-yield checks are intensity, duration, timing (per the Tier A row).
Tier 4 — Lifestyle Factors: Caffeine, Alcohol, Exercise, Naps ★★★★

Sleep Med Rev 2023;69:101764. Read here.
Key findingCaffeine reduced total sleep time by 45 minutes and sleep efficiency by 7%. Recommended cutoff of ≥8.8 hours before bedtime for a standard cup (~107 mg). Older adults more sensitive due to slower CYP1A2 metabolism.

BMC Geriatrics 2025. Read here.

Gardiner C, Weakley J, Burke LM et al. The Effect of Alcohol on Subsequent Sleep in Healthy Adults: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis.
Sleep Med Rev 2025;80:102030. Read here.
