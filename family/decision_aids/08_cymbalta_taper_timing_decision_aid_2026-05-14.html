Recommendation

After. Wait until ketamine response is established at 2–3 sessions (approximately 1.5 weeks under the twice-weekly IV protocol) before initiating any Cymbalta taper. Starting the taper concurrent with the May 18 ketamine restart is not supported by the published evidence and would compromise both the ketamine response assessment and Dad's tolerability. Julane's instinct that ketamine can support medication transitions is reasonable, but the evidence supporting that idea is benzodiazepine-specific — it doesn't extend to SNRIs like Cymbalta. The confirmation that the protocol is twice-weekly IV ketamine (rather than primarily sublingual lozenges) actually shortens the assessment window — the Singh 2016 RCT specifically demonstrated meaningful response under this dosing schedule — making the cost of waiting modest.

Why this matters for Dad specifically

Dad's documented February 2026 dramatic ketamine response occurred on Cymbalta 60 mg + brexpiprazole 1 mg. The May 18 restart is essentially a re-creation of that condition (now with brexpiprazole stopped) — but with one key shift: the route will be twice-weekly IV ketamine with occasional at-home lozenges, rather than the primarily sublingual protocol of the February course. IV ketamine has 100% bioavailability (vs. ~25–30% for sublingual), larger acute effect sizes, and is the route with the strongest published maintenance RCTs (Singh 2016, Phillips 2019). This matters here because response assessment is cleaner and faster with IV — 2–3 sessions at twice-weekly dosing is approximately 1.5 weeks, with a more reliable signal than slower sublingual titration would provide. Tapering Cymbalta concurrent with the restart introduces multiple destabilizing variables at exactly the window where the family most needs that clean read. Cymbalta discontinuation symptoms (dizziness, nausea, brain zaps, anxiety, insomnia) peak at 1–2 weeks — directly overlapping the critical first 2–3 ketamine sessions. Layer this on top of Dad's still-open post-lorazepam rebound window (he finished his Ativan taper roughly a week ago after ~3 weeks of near-daily use; rebound resolution typically takes 1–2 weeks) and there's a real risk of three pharmacological transitions running simultaneously in a 76-year-old — exactly what the geriatric pharmacology literature consistently advises against. The cleaner, evidence-supported sequencing is: restart IV ketamine on the unchanged Cymbalta 60 mg, assess response over 2–3 sessions (1.5 weeks), then plan any antidepressant changes as a separate, monitored intervention with attribution intact.

The evidence
Why deferring the taper is the evidence-supported approach The case for concurrent taper — and why the evidence doesn't support it for Cymbalta
Cymbalta discontinuation symptoms peak in the same window as the critical ketamine assessment. Duloxetine discontinuation syndrome typically peaks at 1–2 weeks after a dose reduction. Symptoms include dizziness, nausea, brain zaps (electric-shock-like sensations in the head), anxiety, irritability, insomnia, and emotional lability. Several of these symptoms overlap with both depressive symptoms and ketamine session side effects — meaning any clinical change during the first 2–3 sessions would be impossible to attribute cleanly to ketamine, withdrawal, or relapse. Julane's framing: ketamine can support antidepressant discontinuation. The clinical instinct is reasonable — ketamine has documented evidence of facilitating benzodiazepine discontinuation. In one 2023 cohort, 91% of TRD patients successfully discontinued all benzodiazepines during a 4-week ketamine course, with 64% remaining off them at 1 year. The mechanism: ketamine's antidepressant and anxiolytic effects can buffer some of the rebound symptoms that make medication withdrawal hard. The framing has real clinical merit — for benzodiazepines.
The twice-weekly IV protocol means the wait isn't long. The Singh 2016 RCT specifically used 0.5 mg/kg twice-weekly IV ketamine and demonstrated meaningful response over 15 days. 2–3 sessions at twice-weekly dosing is approximately 1.5 weeks — about half the wait that would be involved with slower sublingual titration. Phillips 2019 found cumulative effects of repeated IV infusions, with a median of 3 infusions to response. The cost of waiting before initiating a Cymbalta taper is small, and the assessment quality is high. —
No clinical trial has ever paired ketamine induction with a concurrent SNRI taper. The major ketamine and esketamine trials follow one of three designs: (a) wash patients off all antidepressants 2+ weeks before ketamine starts (then ketamine monotherapy); (b) maintain the existing oral antidepressant at a stable dose throughout the induction; or (c) cross-taper to a new oral antidepressant while starting ketamine. None of the published trials introduces an SNRI taper-to-nothing during the induction window. The absence of any trial protocol attempting this is itself informative — trial designers have consistently avoided this design. But the evidence is benzodiazepine-specific. The pharmacology of benzodiazepine withdrawal (GABAergic rebound, anxiety, seizure risk) is fundamentally different from SNRI discontinuation (serotonergic and noradrenergic dysregulation producing brain zaps, dizziness, paresthesias). There is no published evidence that ketamine reduces the severity or duration of SNRI or SSRI discontinuation symptoms specifically. Extrapolating from the benzodiazepine literature to SNRIs is not evidence-supported, even if the intuition is sensible.
Preserving the documented-response condition matters most for a prior responder. Dad had a dramatic ketamine response in February 2026 on Cymbalta + brexpiprazole. The closest the family can get to reproducing that condition now (with brexpiprazole already off) is keeping Cymbalta unchanged. Tapering Cymbalta concurrent with the restart introduces an additional variable that wasn't present during the documented response — changing the medication context in which the family knows ketamine worked. Concurrent taper hasn't been studied — but it also hasn't been shown to be unsafe. Some clinicians do use ketamine as a "soft landing" for various medication transitions in real-world practice, based on the same clinical instinct Julane is operating from. This is real practice-level experience, even if it doesn't appear in trial protocols. The honest version is: it's not evidence-supported, but it's also not been shown to be harmful — just unstudied.
The Del Casale 2025 large-cohort study (n=55,480) showed SNRI + ketamine outperforms SSRI + ketamine on mortality and relapse. The SNRI advantage was on hard outcomes: 5.3% mortality vs. 9.1%, 14.8% depression relapse vs. 21.2%. The authors hypothesized that the dual mechanism of SNRIs may synergize with ketamine's glutamatergic effects. This finding argues for keeping the SNRI in place during the ketamine restart period, not removing it. For Dad's RBD profile, eventually getting off the SNRI may be beneficial. There is real reason to think Dad's REM Sleep Behavior Disorder may be antidepressant-associated (frequency decreasing on long-term SSRIs/SNRIs is more consistent with that pattern than with idiopathic RBD). Removing the SNRI could plausibly reduce RBD episodes further. The question isn't whether to consider tapering — it's when. The evidence supports doing this as a separate, sequenced intervention later, not concurrent with the May 18 restart.
Layered transitions in a 76-year-old are a known geriatric-pharmacology hazard. Dad finished his lorazepam taper 6 days ago and is still in the typical 1–2 week post-benzodiazepine rebound window. Adding a Cymbalta taper concurrent with the ketamine restart would mean three overlapping pharmacological transitions in the same 2-week window: post-benzodiazepine rebound, SNRI discontinuation, ketamine induction. The geriatric literature consistently emphasizes sequential, not simultaneous, medication changes — to allow attribution of adverse effects and to minimize cumulative destabilization. Speed of medication transition is a clinical-priority question, not just an evidence question. If the family decides that getting Dad off serotonergic medications is a high priority — for instance, because of the RBD concerns or the prodromal Lewy-spectrum question — the speed of the transition may matter to them. The evidence-supported sequencing means a slightly slower path (taper starts at week 2–3 of ketamine, not at week 0), but this is a few weeks of delay in service of a cleaner clinical read. It is not an indefinite postponement.
The TRANSFORM-3 trial in adults ≥65 already showed attenuated ketamine response in this age group. Esketamine + new oral antidepressant did not significantly outperform placebo + oral antidepressant in this age range. The implication for Dad: late-life ketamine response is more conditional than younger-adult ketamine response, and the family should optimize every controllable variable in service of getting a clean response — which argues against introducing an unnecessary destabilization concurrent with the restart.  
Strength of evidence for deferring the taper until ketamine response is established: ★★★★★
Convergence across multiple lines of evidence: every major ketamine trial avoided concurrent SNRI taper; duloxetine discontinuation peaks exactly during the critical ketamine assessment window; the Del Casale 2025 large-cohort data favor SNRI continuation alongside ketamine; geriatric pharmacology consistently advises against layered transitions; and the documented-response condition is preserved by keeping Cymbalta at 60 mg.
What both approaches share
  • Both approaches accept that Cymbalta will likely come off eventually. The disagreement is about timing, not about whether to taper at all. Dad's RBD picture, the prodromal Lewy-spectrum questions, and the long-term plan all point to revisiting his antidepressant regimen — the question is just whether to do that concurrent with the May 18 restart or as a separate intervention 4–6 weeks later.
  • Both approaches respect Julane's underlying clinical intuition. The idea that ketamine can support medication transitions is real and well-evidenced for benzodiazepines. The disagreement is whether that finding extends to SNRIs without published evidence to support the extension. Julane's view is defensible clinical extrapolation; the published evidence simply hasn't followed up on it for SNRIs specifically.
  • A slow taper is preferable in either timing. If/when the taper is initiated — whether at May 18 or at week 3 — current geriatric guidance recommends reducing duloxetine by ~25% every 2–4 weeks, with close monitoring for discontinuation symptoms and depressive worsening. Hyperbolic tapering (slower-than-linear reductions, especially near the bottom of the dose range) may further reduce withdrawal severity.
  • The neurology evaluation in June and the MMA recheck on August 6 are real decision points. Both could materially change the antidepressant strategy. Deferring the taper preserves the option to make this decision with more information.

If ketamine response is re-established (≥50% reduction in depressive symptoms or clinically meaningful improvement), then plan a slow Cymbalta taper as a separate intervention: ~25% reductions every 2–4 weeks, with close monitoring for discontinuation symptoms (DESS criteria) and any depressive recurrence.
  4. If ketamine response is not re-established, hold Cymbalta and reassess the entire treatment plan with Dr. Rubin, the K Clinic psychiatrist, and Julane before introducing any additional medication changes.

A short conversation now is better than discovering the disagreement on May 18.

Tier 2 — Major RCTs ★★★★
Daly EJ, Trivedi MH et al. Efficacy of Esketamine Nasal Spray Plus Oral Antidepressant Treatment for Relapse Prevention in Patients With Treatment-Resistant Depression (SUSTAIN-1).
JAMA Psychiatry 2019;76:893–903. Read here.
Key findingThe landmark ketamine relapse-prevention trial. Patients on continued esketamine + oral antidepressant had 51% lower relapse than those on antidepressant alone. The oral antidepressant was maintained throughout — no protocol involved tapering it to nothing during ketamine treatment.
How it appliesThe strongest evidence base for ketamine maintenance keeps the oral antidepressant stable. No trial has paired ketamine induction with concurrent SNRI taper — the trial designers consistently avoid that design.
Janik A, Qiu X et al. Esketamine Monotherapy in Adults With Treatment-Resistant Depression.
JAMA Psychiatry 2025;82:877–887. Read here.
Key findingFirst placebo-controlled RCT of esketamine monotherapy (after 2+ week antidepressant-free period). Both 56 mg and 84 mg doses outperformed placebo at day 28. Notably, the trial design required a complete antidepressant washout BEFORE ketamine started — not a concurrent taper during induction. The FDA Spravato label has subsequently been updated to include monotherapy as an indication.
How it appliesIf the eventual plan is to get Dad off SNRIs entirely, the published trial design supports doing this as a wash-then-restart sequence (taper completely, then begin ketamine fresh) — not as a concurrent taper during an active induction.
Singh JB, Fedgchin M et al. A Double-Blind, Randomized, Placebo-Controlled, Dose-Frequency Study of Intravenous Ketamine in Patients With Treatment-Resistant Depression.
Am J Psychiatry 2016;173:816–826. Read here.
Key findingRCT (n=67) of IV ketamine 0.5 mg/kg twice weekly vs. three times weekly vs. placebo. Twice-weekly IV ketamine produced 69% response and 37.5% remission rates over 15 days, with durable antidepressant effects through the maintenance period.
How it appliesValidates the twice-weekly IV protocol Dad will receive — and establishes that meaningful response can be assessed within ~15 days under this regimen. This is the basis for "wait 2–3 sessions" being a short and high-quality assessment window rather than an open-ended delay.
Phillips JL, Norris S et al. Single, Repeated, and Maintenance Ketamine Infusions for Treatment-Resistant Depression.
Am J Psychiatry 2019;176:401–409. Read here.
Key findingRepeated IV ketamine infusions showed cumulative antidepressant effects with a median of 3 infusions to response. Patients on scheduled maintenance had ~27% relapse vs. ~46% with variable protocols.
How it appliesThe "3 infusions to response" median means Dad's response read at 2–3 twice-weekly IV sessions is on solid evidence-based ground — and that maintaining the response requires the structured (not ad hoc) protocol he's planned to be on. Supports keeping the medication context stable through this assessment window.
Tier 3 — Systematic Reviews and Real-World Cohorts ★★★
Del Casale A et al. Esketamine Combined With SSRI or SNRI for Treatment-Resistant Depression.
JAMA Psychiatry 2025;82:810–817. Read here.
Key findingPropensity-matched cohort, n=55,480. SNRI + esketamine had lower mortality (5.3% vs. 9.1%), lower hospitalization (0.1% vs. 0.2%), and lower depression relapse (14.8% vs. 21.2%) than SSRI + esketamine. The authors hypothesized that the dual mechanism of SNRIs may synergize with ketamine's glutamatergic effects.

If the eventual decision is to taper anyway (for RBD or Lewy-spectrum reasons), this should be a separate, sequenced intervention, not concurrent with the May 18 restart.
Kalfas M, Tsapekos D et al. Incidence and Nature of Antidepressant Discontinuation Symptoms.
JAMA Psychiatry 2025;82:896–904. Read here.
Key findingMeta-analysis of antidepressant discontinuation symptoms. Duloxetine produced approximately 1.6 additional symptoms on the standard discontinuation symptom scale at week 1 compared to placebo (standardized effect size 0.40). Most common symptoms: dizziness (~6% risk difference), nausea (~3%), plus headache, paresthesia, irritability, insomnia, anxiety, and hyperhidrosis. Peak symptoms typically at 1–2 weeks post-discontinuation.

Tier 4 — Mechanism & Risk-Side Trials ★★
Garel N et al. Intravenous Ketamine for Benzodiazepine Deprescription and Withdrawal Management in Treatment-Resistant Depression.
Neuropsychopharmacology 2023;48:1769–1777. Read here.
Key findingIn a cohort of TRD patients undergoing ketamine treatment, 91% successfully discontinued all benzodiazepines during a 4-week ketamine course; 64% remained abstinent at 1-year follow-up.
How it appliesThis is the evidence Julane's clinical instinct is drawing from — and it is real and well-documented. The critical caveat: the finding is benzodiazepine-specific. Extending this evidence to Cymbalta would be reasonable clinical extrapolation but is not evidence-supported.
