The honest balance on ECT for Dad's profile

A revised reading of the published evidence (per OE consultation #19, 5/20). The earlier draft of this handout overstated the case against ECT; this revision is more balanced. ECT is a reasonable option for Dad, with both meaningful upsides and real but manageable downsides.

What favors ECT in Dad's profile
  • Robust late-life severe TRD evidence: Kellner PRIDE 2016 (n=240, age ≥60, right unilateral ultrabrief pulse + venlafaxine) showed 61.7% remission at end of acute course. Late-life ECT response rates: 60–70%.
  • Specifically in Lewy-spectrum disease: Morikawa 2024 (n=25 confirmed DLB / prodromal DLB with abnormal DAT-SPECT) showed 96% at least some improvement, 60% remarkable improvement. Parkinsonism actually improved with ECT. No cognitive worsening. This is the most directly applicable safety data for Dad.
  • Takamiya 2021 meta-analysis of ECT in Parkinson's disease (14 studies, n=129): improved both motor and non-motor symptoms without cognitive worsening. No documented cases of ECT precipitating motor decline in prodromal-synucleinopathy.
  • Fast onset: response typically detectable within 1–2 weeks. Faster than starting any new oral antidepressant.
  • Cognitive cost is largely transient in responders. ELEKT-D showed 8.8-point delayed-recall decrement at end of treatment, but Kumpf 2025 secondary analysis showed differences resolved among responders at 1-, 3-, and 6-month follow-up.
  • If Dad doesn't feel up for another ketamine session, ECT is a legitimate alternative — not a last resort, just a different treatment with its own evidence base.
What argues for caution or for ketamine-first
  • ELEKT-D 2023 (n=403): ketamine non-inferior to ECT for nonpsychotic TRD with significant cognitive-safety advantage during the treatment course. If Dad is open to another ketamine attempt, this argues for trying that first.
  • Documented prior ketamine response (Feb 2026, ~4-week dramatic response). N=1 prior response is the single strongest predictor of re-response. The May 18 Royal Oak session was difficult but is not a fairly-tested ketamine course at A2.
  • GA exposure considerations. Per-ECT-session GA is brief (~5–10 min TIVA) and lower per-exposure risk than the March 90-min surgical GA. Cumulative across induction (6–12 sessions) is modest. Maintenance ECT (20–40 sessions/year if pursued) is where cumulative-dose concerns become meaningful — though this only arises if induction is successful.
  • The March 2026 post-GA recovery has been imperfect. Whether the deterioration was POD/POCD or depression relapse + life situational + ketamine cessation is genuinely unclear. Either interpretation suggests some elevated vulnerability — but the magnitude is uncertain.
  • The pending DAT-SPECT would materially refine the picture. If abnormal, it confirms Dad in the Morikawa 2024 cohort (good ECT outcomes). If normal, the prodromal-synucleinopathy concern attenuates. Worth a few weeks' wait if clinically feasible.
  • Acuity does not currently meet the published threshold for ECT urgency (suicidality, catatonia, psychosis, food refusal). This doesn't argue against ECT — it argues that the family has time to do the workup and decision-making properly rather than under emergency pressure.
How severe are the GA exposures, really?

A more precise read than the earlier version of this handout offered:

  • Per ECT session: ~5–10 minutes of total intravenous anesthesia (propofol or methohexital + succinylcholine for muscle relaxation). No volatile inhalation agents, no surgical inflammation, no blood loss, no post-op opioids. Per-exposure cognitive risk is materially lower than what a 60–90 minute surgical GA (like the March Aquablation) represents. Not zero — but not in the same magnitude.
  • Cumulative across an induction course (6–12 sessions): Total GA time ~30–120 minutes across 3–4 weeks. The MAAS cohort's cumulative-time signal applies but at the lower end of the dose-response. The acute cognitive cost during the course (ELEKT-D's 8.8-point delayed-recall decrement) largely resolves in responders by 1 month.
  • Cumulative across induction + a year of maintenance: 20–40 brief GA exposures total. This is the actually-concerning cumulative dose, but it only arises if Dad responds and is committed to maintenance. Maintenance frequency can be adjusted; many patients taper to monthly or less; some don't need maintenance at all. This is a downstream question, not a present-decision question.
  • The Dad-specific risk multiplier: Kim 2018 found RBD as POD predictor (OR 1.45) in elderly surgical patients. Dad's prodromal-synucleinopathy profile theoretically raises POD risk. But Morikawa 2024 — the population that overlaps Dad's profile most — found 24% transient delirium rate and no serious AEs across the full course. The Dad-specific multiplier is real but not large.

A decision frame that may help
  • If Dad actively wants to try ECT (or feels more able to face it than another ketamine session): the evidence supports proceeding, with attention to technique (right unilateral ultrabrief pulse, propofol-based anesthesia, cognitive baseline and monitoring). The Morikawa 2024 data is reassuring for his profile.
  • If Dad is open to either path: the published evidence and his prior responder status modestly favor completing the A2 ketamine course first, with ECT held as the next step if the ketamine course doesn't produce a sustained response. The reasoning is the cognitive-safety advantage in ELEKT-D and the no-GA-required nature of ketamine, not any catastrophic-risk concern about ECT.
  • If Dad actively wants to defer ECT and try ketamine: the A2 restart on June 8 is the active path; the U-M ECT team can be a held-in-reserve resource if ketamine doesn't work.
  • What probably shouldn't drive the decision: family preference, the desire to "do something" after the difficult 5/18 session, or unstated assumptions about which treatment is the "real" intervention. Both ECT and ketamine are real interventions with real evidence; the question is which fits Dad's preferences and situation best.
A consideration that may matter regardless of which path is chosen

Completing the DAT-SPECT and the U-M Movement Disorders evaluation in the next few weeks would meaningfully refine the picture either way: if Dad ends up doing ECT, abnormal DAT-SPECT confirms him in the Morikawa cohort (favorable outcomes) and informs technique; if Dad ends up doing ketamine, the same workup informs the depakote question and the longer-term treatment trajectory. The workup is not an obstacle to either treatment path — it's information that improves whichever path is chosen. Whether ECT can wait the 1–3 weeks for the DAT-SPECT result is a reasonable thing to ask the U-M team.

Key studies + post-consultation follow-up

ECT in Lewy-spectrum and Parkinson's disease — favorable signal
Morikawa F, Kobayashi R, Murayama T et al. Evaluating ECT for Dementia With Lewy Bodies, Including the Prodromal Stage.
Int J Geriatr Psychiatry 2024;39:e70020.
Key findingRetrospective ECT analysis in 25 DLB / prodromal DLB / MCI-LB patients (all with abnormal DAT-SPECT and MIBG). 96% at least minimal improvement; 60% remarkable improvement. Parkinsonism improved with ECT (mean Hoehn-Yahr 1.8). Adverse events: 24% transient delirium, 4% transient amnesia; no serious AEs. No cognitive worsening before vs after ECT. Most directly applicable safety data for Dad's profile.
Takamiya A et al. ECT for Parkinson's Disease: Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis.
Mov Disord 2021;36:50–58.
Key finding14 studies, n=129 PD patients. ECT significantly improved both motor and non-motor symptoms without worsening cognitive functioning. No documented cases of ECT precipitating motor decline.
Kellner CH et al. Right Unilateral Ultrabrief Pulse ECT in Geriatric Depression (PRIDE Phase 1).
Am J Psychiatry 2016;173:1101–1109.
Key findingn=240, age ≥60. RUL-UBP ECT plus venlafaxine: 61.7% remission in geriatric depression. Establishes ECT's real efficacy in late-life TRD with the technique that minimizes cognitive side effects.
Ketamine comparison + cognitive safety
Anand A et al. Ketamine vs ECT for Nonpsychotic Treatment-Resistant Major Depression (ELEKT-D).
N Engl J Med 2023;388:2315–2325.
Key findingn=403. Ketamine non-inferior to ECT on response (55.4% vs 41.2%). Delayed-recall T-score change: −0.9 (ketamine) vs −9.7 (ECT) at end of treatment — meaningful cognitive-safety advantage for ketamine during the treatment course.
Kumpf KT et al. Cognitive Effects of Repeated IV Ketamine and ECT in TRD (ELEKT-D Secondary Analysis).
J Clin Psychiatry 2025.
Key findingECT cognitive cost largely transient — differences between groups resolved among responders at 1-, 3-, and 6-month follow-up. The cognitive concern is during the treatment course, not durable in responders.
General anesthesia exposure considerations
Schulte PJ et al. Anaesthesia, Surgery, and Long-Term Cognitive Trajectories (Mayo Clinic Study of Aging).
Br J Anaesth 2018;121:398–405.
Key findingn=1,819 adults ≥50. Single new GA+surgery exposure → 0.2 SD additional cognitive decline over 5 years. Real but modest signal; applies most strongly to longer surgical GA, less cleanly to brief ECT-anesthesia.
Kunicki ZJ et al. Six-Year Cognitive Trajectory After Major Surgery and Delirium (SAGES).
JAMA Intern Med 2023;183:442–450.
Key findingPostoperative delirium associated with 40% accelerated cognitive decline slope. "Delirium signals underlying brain vulnerability." The framework is conceptually important even if Dad's March course was not formally delirium.

A note on the family role

The family's role in this decision is to make sure Dad has the relevant information and that the U-M team has the relevant context — not to push toward a particular outcome. ECT is a reasonable treatment for Dad's severity and profile. Another ketamine course is also a reasonable treatment. The right answer depends on Dad's preferences, his appetite for each modality given recent experiences, the team's clinical judgment, and the eventual results of the workup. The family's job is to support whichever path Dad and the team choose — and to ensure the chosen path is executed with appropriate technique, monitoring, and follow-through.
