Where things stand

Dad is in the worst acute episode anyone has seen in some years. Most of it is probably reversible. The motor signs, sleep-movement disorder, daytime sleepiness, and 1–2 year appetite-recognition change together need neurological evaluation — not to pre-diagnose, but because those features are not fully explained by depression alone, and neurology can distinguish among the candidate causes.

The February ketamine response is the single most useful clinical data point we have: it establishes that his cortical circuits are still capable of responding. Restart is a top priority.

More context — what's driving the current state (candidate contributors, not confirmed causes)

Medical / pharmacological: possible lithium toxicity (recent start, weight loss, elderly); olanzapine 5mg started in the last few weeks for anxiety, coinciding precisely with the acute decompensation (likely drug-induced parkinsonism); ~5 weeks post-general-anesthesia (post-op cognitive tail); ketamine-relapse after February cessation; possible SIADH from Cymbalta; alcohol at the wedding.

Life-situational: multi-week loss of exercise (a documented mood regulator for him); anticipatory anxiety about the prostate procedure; post-surgical recovery stress; loss of mood-regulating routines; sleep disruption from post-procedural urinary inflammation; the wedding weekend itself as peak acute stressor. The parsimonious framing worth holding open: an elderly chronically-depressive high-anxiety man had a hard month with multiple ordinary stressors compounding plus a recently-added antipsychotic that itself explains much of the motor and cognitive picture.

🚨 Red flags

Same-day ER or doctor call if:
  • Sudden worsening of tremor, confusion, vomiting, diarrhea, or unsteadiness (possible lithium toxicity)
  • Any fall with head strike
  • New confusion beyond baseline, fever, or painful urination (UTIs in elderly often present as sudden confusion)
  • Any mention of self-harm
  • Chest pain, shortness of breath, new irregular heartbeat
  • Severe weakness, seizures, or loss of consciousness
Watch and report (not emergency):
  • Further weight loss
  • Seeing things that aren't there
  • Worse sleep-movement episodes or injuries during sleep
  • Dizziness on standing, falls without head strike
  • Withdrawing more than current baseline

Clinical decisions

  • Call the prescribing psychiatrist. Ask for four things plus a sequencing conversation:
    • Stop lithium today and draw a lithium blood level within 24 hours.
    • Bedside lorazepam challenge (1–2 mg injection) for possible catatonia — a one-time diagnostic/therapeutic intervention.
    • Start a gradual olanzapine taper. Do not add a replacement medication in parallel — goal is to return him to pre-olanzapine baseline first, then reassess.
    • Deprescribe the standing lorazepam PRN once the catatonia challenge is complete. Propranolol 10–20 mg PRN offered as a non-CNS-depressant alternative if a standing tool is wanted during the taper.
    • Sequencing: one variable at a time. Order: (a) lithium stop + catatonia challenge today, (b) olanzapine taper + lorazepam deprescribe, nothing added in parallel, (c) ketamine restart with maintenance built in, (d) reassess at a clean off-olanzapine, off-lorazepam baseline, (e) Cymbalta taper considered later.
    • Watch for serotonin-syndrome signs during the olanzapine taper (tremor, clonus, hyperreflexia, autonomic lability) — olanzapine has been buffering Cymbalta's serotonergic load.
  • Confirm what was on the recent blood panel. If lithium level wasn't included, order today. Add any missing items: sodium + serum osmolality (SIADH screen), iron panel with ferritin + transferrin saturation, kidney function, thyroid, calcium, B12 + methylmalonic acid + homocysteine, vitamin D, CBC, LFTs. See Cleveland Clinic on lithium toxicity.
  • IV thiamine 100 mg empirically via urgent care or PCP. Zero-downside Wernicke prophylaxis given recent alcohol + weight loss.
